SIP Calculator for India — Calculate Mutual Fund Returns Free

April 27, 2026 • 5 min read

A Systematic Investment Plan (SIP) is the most popular way Indians invest in mutual funds — and for good reason. By investing a fixed amount every month (as little as ₹500), you benefit from rupee-cost averaging and the power of compounding, building wealth without timing the market. Whether you are a first-time investor or a seasoned one, understanding how SIP returns are calculated helps you set realistic goals and stay the course during market volatility.

How SIP Returns Are Calculated

SIP returns cannot be calculated with a simple annualised formula because each instalment is invested at a different time and earns returns for a different duration. The correct method is the Extended Internal Rate of Return (XIRR), which accounts for the timing of every cash flow.

For a quick approximation, the future value formula works: FV = PMT × (((1 + r)^n − 1) / r) × (1 + r), where PMT is the monthly investment, r is the monthly interest rate (annual rate ÷ 12), and n is the number of months.

Example: ₹5,000/month for 10 years at 12% annual return. r = 12/12/100 = 0.01. n = 120. FV ≈ ₹11.6 lakh invested, ₹11.6 lakh in gains, total maturity value approximately ₹23.2 lakh. Your money more than doubles purely through the compounding effect.

Rupee Cost Averaging Explained

One of the most powerful (and misunderstood) benefits of SIP is rupee cost averaging. Because you invest a fixed rupee amount every month regardless of market conditions, you automatically buy more units when markets fall and fewer units when markets rise.

Example: You invest ₹1,000/month in a fund. Month 1: NAV ₹50 → you buy 20 units. Month 2: NAV ₹40 (market dips) → you buy 25 units. Month 3: NAV ₹50 (market recovers) → you buy 20 units. Total units = 65. Total invested = ₹3,000. Average cost per unit = ₹3,000 ÷ 65 = ₹46.15.

The current NAV is ₹50, but your average cost is only ₹46.15 — you are already in profit even though the market is exactly where it started. This is the magic of rupee cost averaging: market dips work in your favour rather than against you.

SIP vs Lump Sum

Both strategies have their place. A lump sum investment is mathematically optimal when you invest at a market low — every rupee benefits from the full recovery. However, almost nobody can consistently predict market lows.

SIP is better suited for regular salary earners who cannot time the market. Research on Indian markets shows that SIP delivers approximately 85–90% of lump sum returns in sustained bull markets but performs significantly better than lump sum in volatile or falling markets, because the averaging mechanism protects you from deploying all your capital at a peak.

The most important advantage of SIP is behavioural: it enforces discipline. When markets crash 30%, most investors panic and stop investing. A standing SIP instruction removes emotion from the equation — your investment continues automatically at the lowest prices.

Tax Treatment of SIP Returns

Each SIP instalment is treated as a separate investment for tax purposes, with its own holding period. Here is what you need to know:

  • Equity Mutual Funds (held > 1 year): Long-Term Capital Gains (LTCG) at 12.5% on gains above ₹1.25 lakh per financial year. Units redeemed before 1 year attract Short-Term Capital Gains (STCG) at 20%.
  • Debt Mutual Funds (from April 2023 onwards): All gains — regardless of holding period — are added to your income and taxed at your applicable slab rate.
  • ELSS (Equity Linked Savings Scheme): Tax deduction under Section 80C up to ₹1.5 lakh per year. Mandatory 3-year lock-in per instalment. Best of both worlds: tax saving on the way in and LTCG treatment on the way out. This is the most tax-efficient SIP option for most investors.

How to Choose the Right SIP Amount

A common rule of thumb is to save and invest at least 20% of your take-home income. To work backwards from a goal: decide your target corpus and timeline, then use a SIP calculator to find the required monthly investment.

The cost of delay: ₹5,000/month started at age 25 grows to approximately ₹1.75 crore by age 60 at 12% return (35 years). The same ₹5,000/month started at age 35 grows to only approximately ₹50 lakh (25 years). A 10-year delay shrinks your final corpus to less than one-third — not one-half — because compounding is exponential, not linear.

Even small increases help dramatically. A step-up SIP — increasing your monthly investment by 10% every year — can nearly double your final corpus compared to a flat SIP, because you invest more in the years when your salary is highest and compounding has the most time to work.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the minimum amount I can invest in a SIP?

Most mutual funds in India accept SIP investments starting from ₹500 per month. Some funds like ELSS (tax-saving funds) may require ₹500 or ₹1,000 as the minimum. There is no maximum limit — you can invest any amount. Many AMCs (Asset Management Companies) also allow weekly or quarterly SIP frequencies, not just monthly.

Can I pause or stop a SIP mid-way?

Yes. Most AMCs allow you to pause a SIP for 1–3 months without cancelling it — useful if you face a temporary cash crunch. You can also stop a SIP permanently at any time without any penalty. Your existing units continue to stay invested and keep earning returns. You can restart the SIP whenever you wish. Pausing or stopping does not trigger any tax event — tax is only applicable when you redeem (sell) units.

Is SIP return guaranteed?

No. SIP is an investment method, not a fixed-return product. Returns depend on the performance of the underlying mutual fund scheme, which is linked to market performance. Equity mutual fund SIPs have historically delivered 12–15% CAGR over 10+ year periods in India, but past returns do not guarantee future results. For guaranteed returns, products like PPF (7.1%) or bank FDs (6.5–7.5%) are appropriate, though they deliver lower long-term returns than equity.
