WebP to JPG: how to convert WebP images free without uploading (2026)

8 min read·April 2026

You downloaded an image from a website. You want to open it in Photoshop, share it on WhatsApp, or attach it to an email — but the file has a .webp extension and nothing opens it. This is increasingly common because Google pushed WebP hard, and most websites now serve it. Here's everything you need to know about WebP and how to convert it to JPG instantly.

What is WebP?

WebP is an image format developed by Google, released in 2010 as part of the VP8 video codec project. It supports both lossy and lossless compression, transparency (like PNG), and even animation (like GIF). The key advantage: WebP files are typically 25–35% smaller than JPEG at equivalent visual quality.

Chrome, Firefox, Safari (since 2020), and Edge all support WebP natively, which is why virtually every major website — Google Images, Wikipedia, Flipkart, Amazon — now serves WebP to reduce bandwidth costs and improve page speed scores.

Why you still need JPG

WebP is excellent for the web, but JPG is still the universal format for everything else:

  • Compatibility: Older apps, Windows Photo Viewer (Windows 7/8), many printers, and some social media platforms only accept JPG/PNG.
  • Editing: Some image editors (Lightroom, older Photoshop versions, many mobile editors) don't support WebP natively.
  • Email attachments: Email clients sometimes strip or refuse non-standard image formats.
  • Government forms: Indian government portals (Aadhaar, income tax, IRCTC) typically require JPG uploads with specific file size limits.
  • Printing: Print shops and services almost universally expect JPG or TIFF.

What about AVIF?

AVIF (AV1 Image File Format) is even newer than WebP — developed by the Alliance for Open Media in 2019. It offers even better compression than WebP (up to 50% smaller than JPEG) but has less universal software support. You may encounter .avif files from modern web browsers. The same browser-based conversion approach handles AVIF just as easily as WebP.

Quality settings explained

JPG quality is a scale from 1–100. Here's what the numbers mean in practice:

  • 90–100: Near-lossless. Essentially indistinguishable from the original. Larger files. Use for photos you'll edit or print.
  • 75–89: High quality. Good for web and email. Slight file size benefit.
  • 60–74: Visible compression artifacts on close inspection. Good for thumbnails and previews.
  • Below 60: Noticeable quality degradation. Use only when file size is critical.

The default of 92 in our tool is the sweet spot for most users — great quality with modest file size reduction.

Transparency: what happens when you convert WebP to JPG

JPG doesn't support transparency. If your WebP image has a transparent background (common for logos, stickers, product images), converting to JPG will fill that transparency with a colour. The default is white, which is correct for most use cases. If you're placing the image on a dark background, set the background colour in the converter before converting.

If you need to keep transparency, convert to PNG instead — PNG supports alpha channels just like WebP.
