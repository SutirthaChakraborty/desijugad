How to make an AI music video — free, step by step (2026)

15 min read · April 2026

Until very recently, making a music video required either a film crew or a lot of creative patience with stock footage and a video editor. AI changed that. In 2026, you can go from an audio file to a finished MP4 music video — with cuts that actually follow the beat — in under an hour, for a few dollars or less.

This guide covers the full process end to end: how to pick a video model, how beat detection works, what the real cost is at each step, and how to do all of it without giving up your audio file or your API key to anyone you don't trust. We'll use Desi Jugad's AI Music Video Maker as the example, but the concepts apply to any Replicate-based setup.

The basic idea

Making an AI music video is a three-stage process:

  1. Audio prep — Get a public URL for your audio file (required so the video model can access it).
  2. Clip generation — Use a text-to-video model to generate short clips (8–15 seconds) matching your visual concept.
  3. Assembly — Concatenate the clips, replace the model's generated audio with your original track, and export as a single MP4.

The hard part, historically, was step 3 — video editing software, codec knowledge, timeline management. With ffmpeg.wasm (a WebAssembly port of ffmpeg that runs in your browser), that step now takes about 30 seconds and requires zero setup.

Step 1: Prepare your audio

MP3, WAV, and M4A all work. The main constraint is that AI video models can't receive audio directly from your browser — they need a public URL they can fetch from their own servers. This means your audio needs to be temporarily hosted somewhere publicly accessible.

Two good options:

  • tmpfiles.org — Free, anonymous, no account needed. Upload a file and get a direct URL that expires in 1 hour. Perfect for this use case because you only need the URL for the duration of your generation.
  • file.io — Free, no account, one-download link, 14-day TTL. Good as a fallback.

Desi Jugad's music video tool does this upload automatically when you click Generate — it tries tmpfiles.org first, falls back to file.io if that fails, and shows you the public URL and expiry time so you know exactly what's happening with your audio.

If your audio is already publicly hosted (say, it's a Suno-generated track), the URL is already there and no re-upload is needed. This is why the tool has a "From Music Studio" tab that pulls in your recent Suno generations directly.

Step 2: Choose the right video model

This is the decision that most affects both quality and cost. In April 2026, the main options on Replicate are:

Seedance 2.0 (ByteDance)

Currently the best all-rounder for music videos specifically. It generates video with native audio and produces coherent visual storytelling across a clip — important for music videos, where you want motion and visual rhythm to feel intentional, not random. Cost is around $0.14 per second of output (about ₹12/second). For a 3-minute track using rhythm-cut mode (10 clips × 10 seconds), that's roughly $14 / ₹1,200.

Recommendation: use Seedance 2.0 when your track has vocals and you want the video to feel like it was made for the song.

Veo 3.1 Fast (Google)

Google's video model produces genuinely cinematic output with exceptional lighting and camera movement. Native audio is included. The "Fast" variant cuts the cost to $0.25/second while keeping most of the quality. For atmospheric or instrumental tracks — landscapes, abstract visuals, nature footage — Veo produces better-looking clips than anything else currently available. Cost: around $0.25/second (about ₹21/second).

Recommendation: use Veo 3.1 Fast for atmospheric, scenic, or non-vocal tracks where visual quality matters more than cost.

Wan 2.7 T2V (open source)

Wan is an open-source model with significantly lower costs: roughly $0.04 per second (about ₹3/second). Quality is lower but adequate for social media. The big advantage: it supports clips up to 15 seconds, so fewer clips are needed for a full track. For a 3-minute track in rhythm-cut mode, you're looking at under $3 total. Good for high-volume use or when you need to cover a full track affordably.

Recommendation: use Wan 2.7 when you need to generate a full 3–4 minute music video on a tight budget.

Step 3: Pick a duration strategy

This is where you make the biggest tradeoff decision. Three strategies:

"Cover every second"

The tool generates clips back-to-back to fill the entire song duration. A 3-minute song generates about 22–23 clips at 8 seconds each. These are then concatenated into one continuous video. Result: a music video where there's no looping or repetition — every second has new footage. Cost: most expensive. For a 3-minute song with Seedance 2.0, that's about $25 (₹2,100). With Wan 2.7, about $7 (₹590).

"Rhythm cuts"

The tool analyses your audio using the Web Audio API to find beat peaks, then generates 6–10 clips at those specific moments and crossfades between them. The rest of the song plays over repeating or transitioning clips. Cost: 60–80% cheaper than full coverage, because you're only generating 10 clips instead of 22+. This usually looks more natural anyway — MTV-era music videos used rapid cuts at beat drops for a reason.

"Single long take"

One clip, up to the model's maximum duration (usually 15 seconds), looped or speed-matched to fit the song. Cheapest option by far — typically $0.60–$2 for the entire track. Best for short songs or when you want a contemplative, slow-moving visual backdrop rather than a traditional cut-driven music video.

How beat detection works

The rhythm-cut strategy uses the Web Audio API — specifically the AnalyserNode — to find the musical peaks in your audio file. Here's the process in plain English:

The algorithm loads your audio into an AudioContext and reads through it in small windows (50ms). For each window, it calculates the energy (sum of squared sample values). It then computes a local average across the surrounding 2-second window, and flags any window that's more than 1.5× the local average as a beat candidate. Finally, it deduplicates candidates that are within 1 second of each other — you don't want two cut points 400ms apart.

The result is a list of timestamps in seconds. The tool then distributes clip starts evenly across these beat positions, asking the video model to generate a clip starting at each one. The model doesn't actually know about the music — it just receives a prompt and generates a clip. The beat alignment happens in the assembly phase, where the tool places cuts at those beat timestamps.

This is the same approach used in open-source beat visualization tools. It works reliably for music with a clear rhythm (most pop, hip-hop, electronic, and folk-based genres). It works less well for ambient music or recordings with very irregular beat patterns — in those cases, the "single long take" or "cover every second" strategies usually produce better results.

Step 4: Write your visual concept

The prompt you write determines what every clip looks like. This is where most first-time users undersell themselves — they write a short vague prompt and get generic results, then conclude AI video isn't good enough yet.

Good music video prompts are specific, visual, and cinematic. Compare:

Weak prompt: "A cool video for a sad song"

Strong prompt: "Neon-lit streets of old Delhi at 2am, empty except for a lone auto-rickshaw, reflected light on wet pavements, melancholy atmosphere, slow camera push forward, shot on 35mm film, warm amber streetlights against deep blue shadows"

The second prompt gives the model specific visual information: setting, time, weather, lighting, camera movement, and an aesthetic reference. The model can generate something coherent from that. The first prompt leaves almost everything to chance.

Some useful concepts to include in your prompt:

  • Setting — where the video takes place
  • Time of day and weather — "golden hour", "overcast", "night rain"
  • Camera movement — "slow zoom in", "handheld shaky", "smooth dolly shot", "aerial view"
  • Lighting style — "soft natural light", "harsh neon", "candle-lit", "cinematic side lighting"
  • Film or aesthetic reference — "Wes Anderson symmetry", "shot on 16mm", "dark music video aesthetic"

Step 5: Assembly and audio sync

Once all video clips are downloaded from Replicate, the tool uses ffmpeg.wasm to stitch them together. This runs entirely in your browser — no server required. The process:

  1. Each clip is written to ffmpeg.wasm's virtual filesystem.
  2. A concat list is built and the clips are merged into a single video file.
  3. Your original audio is overlaid on top, replacing any audio the video model generated.
  4. The final MP4 is exported and offered as a download.

The first time you use the stitching feature, it downloads about 30 MB of ffmpeg.wasm code. This is a WebAssembly binary — compiled C code that runs with near-native performance in your browser. After the first download, it's cached permanently by your browser. Subsequent runs are instant.

Real cost examples

Let's calculate actual costs for a common scenario: a 90-second track.

Scenario A — "Full cover" with Wan 2.7:
90 seconds ÷ 10-second clips = 9 clips
9 clips × 10s × $0.04/s = $3.60 (about ₹300)

Scenario B — "Rhythm cuts" with Veo 3.1 Fast:
8 clips × 8s × $0.25/s = $16 (about ₹1,350)
This gets you genuine cinematic quality at fewer clips.

Scenario C — "Rhythm cuts" with Wan 2.7:

Scenario D — "Single take" with Seedance 2.0:

Cheapest. One looped clip. Works well for short tracks or atmospheric videos.

Privacy: what goes where

This is worth spelling out explicitly:

  • Your Replicate API key is stored in your browser's localStorage under dj_ai_keys_v1. It is never sent to desijugad.co.in — only to Replicate via the CORS proxy, which is a dumb pass-through that reads nothing.
  • Your audio file is uploaded to tmpfiles.org (or file.io) to get a public URL. This is sent to Replicate as an input. The file expires after 1 hour on tmpfiles.org.
  • Your prompts are sent to Replicate as part of the prediction request. Replicate's privacy policy governs what they do with these.
  • The final video is assembled in your browser by ffmpeg.wasm and never leaves your device until you download it.

Common problems and fixes

Clips don't match my music's mood: This is almost always a prompting issue. Be more specific about lighting, setting, and camera movement. Avoid abstract mood descriptions ("dark", "sad") in favour of concrete visual descriptions ("dimly lit", "empty streets at 3am").

Generation takes forever or times out: Replicate can get busy, especially for compute-heavy models like Veo. Try a cheaper model first (Wan 2.7) to verify your setup is working, then switch to Veo once you have the right prompt.

Beat cuts don't feel right: Try switching to "Cover every second" for a smoother result, or use the "Single long take" option and accept a more atmospheric, less cut-driven video.

The video aspect ratio is wrong: All models default to 16:9. If your song has cover art in 1:1 or you want a vertical video for mobile, look for models that support custom aspect ratios (Veo and Kling do).
