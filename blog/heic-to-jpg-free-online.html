Why HEIC exists in the first place

HEIC is based on the HEIF container format and usually uses HEVC compression. In practice, it gives you smaller image sizes than JPG at similar visual quality. Apple adopted it because it saves storage space on phones. If you take a lot of photos, that matters.

The downside is that HEIC is not universally supported. Upload a HEIC photo to an older form, a legacy CMS, or a random printer workflow and you will often hit an error. JPG is still the compatibility king.

When to choose JPG

  • Website uploads: most forms, marketplaces, and support portals accept JPG immediately.
  • Email attachments: JPG previews more reliably in old clients.
  • Messaging apps: some apps convert anyway, so starting with JPG avoids weird surprises.
  • Windows workflows: if a colleague says the file will not open, JPG solves the problem fast.

Does HEIC to JPG hurt quality?

Technically, yes: JPG uses lossy compression. Practically, for everyday sharing, the quality loss is usually tiny. If the image is a precious original you may want to edit later, keep the HEIC version too. Think of JPG as the compatibility copy, not necessarily the archive master.

Why in-browser conversion matters

It is not ideal for family pictures, ID photos, property images, travel photos, or work material. A browser-only tool avoids that step entirely. If privacy matters to you, this is the cleanest setup: no remote storage, no processing queue, no mystery retention policy.
