Why Your Resume Format Matters

Recruiters spend 6 seconds scanning a resume. If yours looks unprofessional or hard to read, it gets rejected before they even see your skills. A well-formatted resume with consistent fonts, clear sections, and strategic spacing dramatically increases interview callbacks.

What Makes a Great Resume

1. Clear Structure

  • Name and contact info at top
  • Professional summary (optional, 2-3 lines)
  • Work experience (most recent first)
  • Education
  • Skills

2. Professional Formatting

  • Clean font (Arial, Calibri, or Georgia)
  • Consistent spacing and margins
  • Single page for 5 years experience, max 2 pages
  • No graphics, colors, or design elements (ATS hostile)

3. Action Verbs and Metrics

Bad: "Responsible for managing social media"
Good: "Increased Instagram followers from 5K to 50K (900% growth) through content strategy and influencer partnerships"

Content Tips by Section

Professional Summary

2-3 lines about who you are and what you do. Tailor it for each job you apply to.

Example: "Data-driven marketer with 5 years experience growing B2B SaaS revenue from $500K to $3M through performance marketing and brand strategy."

Work Experience

For each job, include:

  • Job title
  • Company name
  • Dates (month/year)
  • 3-5 bullet points of achievements (with numbers where possible)

Lead with your biggest achievement, not daily tasks.

Education

  • Degree and major
  • University name
  • Graduation year
  • GPA (if 3.5+, otherwise skip)

Skills

List 6-10 relevant skills. Prioritize by job requirement.

Resume Mistakes That Hurt You

  • Typos and grammar errors — Recruiters interpret this as careless
  • Irrelevant information — "5 years old: Learned to ride bicycle"
  • Unexplained employment gaps — If unemployed, explain it briefly
  • Personal pronouns (I, me, we) — Use action verbs instead
  • Objective statement (dated) — Replace with professional summary
  • Photo (unless requested) — Can invite bias

ATS (Applicant Tracking System) Optimization

75% of large companies use ATS software to screen resumes before a human ever sees them. The ATS parses your resume looking for keywords that match the job description. If your resume doesn't have enough keyword matches, it gets filtered out — regardless of your actual qualifications.

The most common ATS-killing mistakes:

  • Tables and columns: ATS parsers read left-to-right, top-to-bottom. A two-column layout can cause the ATS to merge content from both columns into a garbled string. Use a single-column layout for any resume going through an ATS.
  • Headers and footers: Many ATS systems strip out header and footer content entirely. Never put your contact information, name, or page numbers only in a header/footer.
  • Graphics and icons replacing text: A skills section with star-rating icons or a photo of your face contains no machine-readable text. The ATS sees nothing — your skills vanish.
  • Non-standard section headings: "My Journey" instead of "Work Experience", or "What I Know" instead of "Skills" — ATS systems look for standard headings to categorise content. Non-standard headings cause misclassification.
  • Wrong file format: If the job application requests a Word document (.doc/.docx), submitting a PDF can cause parsing errors in some ATS systems (and vice versa). Always match the requested format.

ATS-friendly format: single column, standard section headings (Work Experience, Education, Skills), plain text-parseable font, keyword-matched content.

Tailoring Your Resume for Each Application

Generic resumes have a 15–20% lower interview rate than tailored ones, according to multiple recruitment industry studies. A one-size-fits-all resume is optimised for no one in particular.

The process takes 15–20 minutes per application and significantly improves response rates:

  1. Copy the job description into a word frequency tool or read it carefully to identify repeated terms.
  2. Find the top 8–10 keywords — specific skills, tools, methodologies, and job titles mentioned.
  3. Ensure your resume contains these exact terms, not synonyms. ATS systems match keywords literally. "Project management" and "managing projects" are not the same string to an ATS.
  4. Rewrite your professional summary (2–3 lines) to mirror the language of the specific role. If the job says "growth marketing", your summary should say "growth marketing" — not "performance marketing" or "digital marketing".
  5. Reorder your skills section to put the most relevant skills first.

Resume Length Rules by Experience Level

Resume length is one of the most debated topics in job searching — and one of the easiest to get wrong:

  • 0–3 years experience: 1 page strictly. You do not have enough career history to justify two pages. Padding your resume to fill a second page with weak content signals poor judgement. Every line should justify its presence.
  • 3–10 years experience: 1–2 pages. One page is fine if everything fits well. Two pages is fine if you genuinely have the content. Never half-fill page 2.
  • 10+ years experience: 2 pages maximum for most industries. Focus on the last 10 years; earlier roles can be listed with minimal detail or omitted if not relevant.
  • Academic/scientific/medical CVs: Different rules apply. CVs (curriculum vitae) are not resumes — they document your full academic record and can be many pages long. This guidance is for industry job applications, not academic positions.

The most common mistake is padding a 1-page resume to fill 2 pages with weak content — extra white space, inflated bullet points, or irrelevant early jobs. Recruiters rate resume quality and relevance, not length. A tight 1-page resume that clearly communicates value will outperform a bloated 2-page one.

Frequently Asked Questions

Should a resume be 1 page or 2 pages?

It depends entirely on your experience level. With 0–3 years of experience, one page is the standard — and trying to fill two pages with thin content is a red flag for recruiters. With 3–10 years, either length is acceptable as long as the content is substantive. With 10+ years, two pages is appropriate for most industry roles — but there's almost never a reason to go beyond two pages for a standard job application. The goal is not to fill pages; it's to make every line count.

What format should I save my resume in — PDF or Word?

Match whatever the job posting requests. If it says "attach your CV", PDF is usually safest because it preserves your formatting exactly across all devices and operating systems. If the application portal says "upload .doc or .docx", use Word — some older ATS systems parse Word documents more reliably than PDFs. When in doubt and no format is specified, PDF is the safer default for human reviewers; Word is safer for ATS parsing.

How do I write a resume with no experience?

Focus on transferable skills and proof of ability rather than job titles. Include: academic projects (especially those with measurable outcomes), internships or volunteer work, freelance or independent projects, relevant coursework, and any extracurricular leadership. Replace the "Work Experience" section with "Projects" or "Experience" if your history is academic. Write strong achievement-focused bullet points for each item — "Built an e-commerce website serving 200+ customers" is far stronger than "Personal project: online store." A skills section listing specific tools (Python, Figma, Excel, etc.) is especially important when work history is thin, as it gives ATS systems something to match against.
