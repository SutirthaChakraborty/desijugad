Instagram image sizes 2026: every format you need

7 min read · Updated April 2026

Instagram introduced its current image size specifications years ago, but the optimal sizes for engagement have shifted as Instagram's algorithm, feed layout, and content types evolved. This guide covers exact pixel dimensions for every Instagram format in 2026 — posts, Stories, Reels, carousels, profile pictures, and ads — plus what actually happens when Instagram compresses your image.

Feed posts: portrait beats square for engagement

The 4:5 portrait format (1080×1350px) takes up about 33% more vertical space in the feed than a square photo. More space = more time a user's eye spends on your post before scrolling. Studies consistently show portrait posts get 10–20% more engagement on average compared to square posts of the same content.

Landscape posts (1.91:1) get the least feed space and the lowest average engagement. Only use landscape when the content itself demands it — panoramas, wide-angle architecture, or cinematic crops.

Instagram will automatically crop your feed post to a 4:5 ratio if it's taller (aspect ratios beyond 4:5 in portrait direction are not supported in the feed). If your image is 9:16 and you post it as a feed photo, it gets cropped. Either post it as a Reel/Story or crop it manually first.

Stories and Reels: the safe zone

Stories and Reels display at 1080×1920px (9:16). However, UI elements overlay the top and bottom of the frame:

  • Top ~250 px: profile picture, username, time, close button
  • Bottom ~250 px: reaction, reply bar, like count (Reels)

The safe zone is the central 1080×1420px. Keep all text, faces, logos, and CTAs inside this zone. Background elements and decorative content can extend to the full 1920px height.

Carousels: keep all slides the same ratio

Instagram carousels support up to 10 slides. Critical rule: all slides must be the same aspect ratio. If the first slide is 4:5 (portrait), all subsequent slides must also be 4:5 or they'll be cropped. For maximum engagement, use 4:5 for product carousels and 1:1 for educational multi-slide posts.

Why Instagram compresses your images — and how to fight it

Instagram re-compresses every uploaded image to reduce storage and CDN costs. This can introduce visible JPEG artefacts, especially on:

  • Images with large solid-colour areas (gradients turn blocky)
  • Text overlays (letters get soft and jagged edges)
  • Images with fine detail (grass, hair, fabric)

To minimise compression damage:

  1. Upload at exactly 1080px wide. Don't upload a 4K image — Instagram will downscale and re-compress it. Export at 1080px wide and let Instagram keep it as-is.
  2. Use sRGB colour profile. Instagram strips colour profiles. If you embed Adobe RGB or Display P3, colours shift unpredictably. Convert to sRGB before uploading.
  3. Save as JPEG at 85–92% quality. High-quality JPEGs survive the second compression cycle better than low-quality ones.
  4. Avoid thin text near the edges. Edge content is hardest hit by JPEG macro-blocks.

Colour space: the detail most guides skip

Colour space affects how your image looks after Instagram processes it. Here's the quick rule: always export in sRGB. If your photo editing app uses a wide gamut profile (Adobe RGB, ProPhoto RGB, Display P3), the colours will look oversaturated on devices that assume sRGB — which is most phones and most social platforms.

On a Mac, you can check the colour profile in Preview: Tools → Show Inspector → More Info Tab → Color Profile. On Windows, right-click the file → Properties → Details → Color space.
