Compound Interest Calculator — See Your Money Grow

Published April 27, 2026

Albert Einstein reportedly called compound interest "the eighth wonder of the world." Whether or not he actually said it, the principle is genuinely remarkable. The difference between simple interest and compound interest, given enough time, is the difference between a comfortable retirement and generational wealth. Understanding compound interest is not optional — it is the foundation on which every meaningful personal finance decision is built.

Simple vs Compound Interest

Simple Interest: I = P × r × t. Interest is calculated only on the original principal. No matter how many years pass, the same base amount earns the same annual interest. ₹1,00,000 at 10% for 10 years = ₹1,00,000 in total interest (ending balance: ₹2,00,000).

Compound Interest: A = P × (1 + r/n)^(n × t). Interest is calculated on the principal plus all previously accumulated interest. Each period's gains become the next period's starting balance. ₹1,00,000 at 10% for 10 years, compounded annually = ₹1,59,374 in total interest (ending balance: ₹2,59,374).

Same principal (₹1 lakh), same rate (10%), same duration (10 years) — but compound interest delivers 59% more money than simple interest. Over 20 years, the gap widens dramatically. This is why bank FDs, mutual funds, PPF, and virtually all serious financial instruments use compound interest rather than simple interest.

Compounding Frequency

The more frequently interest compounds, the higher the effective annual yield. ₹1,00,000 at 10% annual rate for 1 year with different compounding frequencies:

Compounding FrequencyAmount After 1 YearEffective Annual Rate
Annual₹1,10,00010.00%
Semi-annual₹1,10,25010.25%
Quarterly₹1,10,38110.38%
Monthly₹1,10,47110.47%
Daily₹1,10,51610.52%

The difference between annual and daily compounding over one year is only ₹516 on ₹1 lakh — small. But the much bigger variable is time and rate, not compounding frequency. A 12% rate compounded annually beats a 10% rate compounded daily by a wide margin over any meaningful period. Focus on maximising your rate of return first; compounding frequency is a secondary consideration.

The Rule of 72

Divide 72 by the annual interest rate to find approximately how many years it takes to double your money. This shortcut works for any compounding rate:

  • PPF at 7.1%: doubles in 72 ÷ 7.1 = 10.1 years
  • Bank FD at 7%: doubles in 72 ÷ 7 = 10.3 years
  • Equity mutual fund at 12%: doubles in 72 ÷ 12 = 6 years
  • Equity at 15%: doubles in 72 ÷ 15 = 4.8 years
  • Credit card debt at 36%: doubles in 72 ÷ 36 = 2 years

That last figure is why credit card debt is so dangerous. Compound interest works for investors and against debtors with equal mathematical ruthlessness. The same principle that grows your wealth also compounds your debt.

Real Examples in Indian Finance

Here is how compound interest plays out across common Indian financial instruments:

  • Bank FD: ₹5 lakh in an SBI FD at 7% for 5 years (quarterly compounding) = ₹7,01,276. Simple interest would have given only ₹6,75,000.
  • PPF: ₹1.5 lakh/year contributed for 15 years at 7.1% = approximately ₹40.68 lakh. Total invested = ₹22.5 lakh. The tax-free nature of PPF returns makes the effective yield even higher for those in the 30% tax bracket.
  • Mutual Fund SIP: ₹10,000/month for 20 years at 12% CAGR = approximately ₹98.9 lakh. Total invested = ₹24 lakh. Compounding gains = ₹74.9 lakh — more than three times the amount you actually invested.
  • Credit Card debt: An unpaid balance of ₹50,000 at 3%/month (36%/year) with minimum payments only grows to approximately ₹1,20,673 in just 2 years. The debt nearly doubles while you think you are managing it.

Time is the Most Important Variable

Of all the inputs to the compound interest formula — principal, rate, frequency, and time — time is the one with the most disproportionate impact. ₹1 lakh invested at 12% per year:

  • After 10 years: ₹3.1 lakh
  • After 20 years: ₹9.6 lakh
  • After 30 years: ₹29.9 lakh

Going from 10 to 20 years (adding 10 years) multiplies the outcome by 3×. Going from 20 to 30 years (same 10-year addition) multiplies it by another 3×. The early years build the base; the later years generate the massive gains — because by then, a large accumulated corpus is earning interest. Starting 10 years earlier does not just add 10 years of gains; it triples the final outcome. This is why every financial advisor says "start early" — not because they are being cliché, but because the mathematics leaves no other conclusion.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between APR and APY?

APR (Annual Percentage Rate) is the stated nominal interest rate without accounting for compounding within the year. APY (Annual Percentage Yield) — also called the Effective Annual Rate (EAR) — includes the effect of compounding within the year and reflects the true annual return. A 10% APR compounded monthly has an APY of 10.47%. When comparing loan offers, the APR tells you the base rate; when comparing savings or investment returns, the APY tells you what you actually earn. In India, RBI regulations require banks to disclose annualised rates — always check whether the quoted rate is the nominal rate or the effective rate.

How often does PPF compound?

PPF (Public Provident Fund) compounds annually — interest is credited to your account once per year, at the end of each financial year (March 31). However, interest is calculated on the minimum balance in your account between the 5th and the last day of each month. This means that if you deposit money before the 5th of the month, that deposit earns interest for that entire month. If you deposit after the 5th, it does not earn interest for that month. To maximise PPF returns, always deposit your annual contribution before April 5th at the start of each financial year.

Why does monthly compounding give more than annual compounding?

With annual compounding, your interest is calculated and added to your principal once per year. With monthly compounding, it is calculated and added twelve times per year — meaning your balance starts growing earlier in the year, and each subsequent month's interest calculation starts from a slightly higher base. This more frequent "interest on interest" cycle produces a higher effective yield. The mathematical difference over short periods (1 year) is small. Over long periods (20–30 years), the gap grows — but the interest rate and time horizon remain far more impactful than compounding frequency alone. Choosing a higher-return investment that compounds annually is still better than a lower-return investment that compounds daily.
