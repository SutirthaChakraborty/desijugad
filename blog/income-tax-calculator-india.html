Income Tax Calculator India — Calculate Tax for FY 2025-26 Free

April 27, 2026 • 6 min read • Updated for 2025-26

India's income tax system offers taxpayers a choice between two regimes since FY 2020-21: the old regime (with deductions and exemptions) and the new regime (lower rates, fewer deductions). For FY 2025-26, the new regime is now the default — your employer will apply it unless you explicitly opt for the old regime by submitting a declaration. Choosing the right regime can save you tens of thousands of rupees, or cost you the same if chosen wrongly.

New Tax Regime Slabs 2025-26

The new regime offers lower slab rates and a generous standard deduction of ₹75,000 for salaried employees. A critical benefit: the Section 87A rebate makes income up to ₹12 lakh effectively zero tax (after standard deduction).

Standard deduction: ₹75,000 for salaried employees. Section 87A rebate: Tax is nil if total income is ₹12 lakh or less (after standard deduction). A ₹12.75 lakh gross salary (₹12 lakh after ₹75K deduction) effectively pays zero tax under the new regime.

Old Tax Regime Slabs 2025-26

The old regime has higher slab rates but allows a wide range of deductions that can significantly reduce your taxable income if you use them well.

  • Up to ₹2,50,000: Nil
  • ₹2,50,001 – ₹5,00,000: 5%
  • ₹5,00,001 – ₹10,00,000: 20%
  • Above ₹10,00,000: 30%

Plus applicable surcharge and 4% health and education cess on total tax. Key deductions available only in the old regime: Section 80C (up to ₹1.5 lakh for PPF, ELSS, LIC, SCSS, home loan principal); Section 80D (health insurance premiums); HRA exemption; LTA; home loan interest under Section 24(b) (up to ₹2 lakh per year); NPS under 80CCD(1B) (additional ₹50,000 over and above 80C).

Which Regime is Better For You?

New regime is better for: Income under ₹7 lakh (zero or near-zero tax anyway); high income above ₹20 lakh where marginal rates in the old regime are higher; those with minimal tax-saving investments or deductions; people who do not have a home loan or do not pay significant rent.

Old regime is better for: Those with a home loan (₹2 lakh interest deduction per year is very valuable); those paying high rent in metro cities where HRA exemption is substantial; those who actively invest in 80C instruments (PPF, ELSS, LIC); those paying health insurance for themselves and parents (₹25,000 + ₹50,000 = ₹75,000 under 80D).

HRA Calculation

If you rent a home, HRA (House Rent Allowance) is one of the most valuable exemptions under the old regime. The exempt amount is the minimum of these three:

  1. Actual HRA received from employer
  2. 50% of basic salary (metro cities) or 40% of basic (non-metro)
  3. Actual rent paid minus 10% of basic salary

Example (Mumbai): Basic salary ₹50,000/month, HRA received ₹20,000, rent paid ₹18,000. The three values: ₹20,000; ₹25,000 (50% of basic); ₹13,000 (₹18,000 – ₹5,000). Exempt HRA = ₹13,000/month = ₹1,56,000/year. Only available under the old regime.

TDS and Advance Tax

Your employer deducts TDS (Tax Deducted at Source) monthly based on your projected annual income and declared investments. Submit Form 12BB to your employer at the start of each financial year to declare your investments, HRA details, and home loan interest — this reduces monthly TDS deduction.

If you have additional income beyond salary (freelance income, rental income, capital gains from investments): you are required to pay Advance Tax in four instalments — 15% by 15 June, 45% by 15 September, 75% by 15 December, and 100% by 15 March. Missing these deadlines attracts interest under Section 234B and 234C, which adds to your final tax bill at ITR filing time.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I switch between old and new tax regime every year?

Salaried individuals can switch between the old and new regime every year at the time of filing their ITR. However, if you have business income, you can switch only once — from new to old — and cannot switch back. For salaried employees, the practical approach is: ask your employer to apply the regime that minimises your TDS based on your current year's investments, and confirm or switch when you file your final ITR.

What is Section 87A rebate and who gets it?

Section 87A provides a full rebate on income tax — meaning your tax liability becomes zero — if your total taxable income does not exceed ₹7 lakh under the old regime or ₹12 lakh under the new regime (for FY 2025-26). It applies to resident individuals only, not HUFs or companies. The rebate is applied after calculating tax on your income but before adding the 4% health and education cess. Note: the rebate does not apply to special rate incomes like STCG (Short-Term Capital Gains) from equity — those are taxed separately.

What documents do I need when filing ITR?

You need: Form 16 from your employer (salary and TDS details); AIS (Annual Information Statement) and Form 26AS from the income tax portal (shows all TDS deductions and high-value transactions); bank account statements for interest income; capital gains statements from your broker or mutual fund AMC; investment proofs for 80C, 80D deductions if claiming under old regime; rental agreement and landlord PAN if claiming HRA above ₹1 lakh/year. Most of this data is pre-filled in the ITR portal — always verify it against your own records.
