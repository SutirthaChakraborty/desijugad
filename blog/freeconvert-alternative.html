The best FreeConvert alternative in 2026 — actually unlimited, no hidden caps

8 min read·Updated May 2026

FreeConvert is a well-built, legitimate tool that handles over 300 file formats. If you've been using it, you've probably hit the 25-conversion-per-day wall on the free tier — or you've wondered, in the back of your mind, exactly what happens to your files after you upload them. Both are reasonable reasons to look for an alternative.

This comparison is written by the team at Desi Jugad, so we have an obvious stake in the outcome. We'll be upfront about that — and equally upfront about where FreeConvert is genuinely better.

Why people look for FreeConvert alternatives

FreeConvert's free tier is more generous than many competitors. There's no watermark on converted files, no email address required, and the interface is clean. But two friction points push users to look elsewhere:

The 25-conversion-per-day cap. For casual users, 25 is enough. For anyone doing batch work — processing a folder of images, handling a stack of PDFs, running a project that needs repeated conversions — the cap becomes an obstacle within hours. When you hit it, your options are to wait until midnight (UTC) or pay $9.99/month for FreeConvert Premium.

The server upload. FreeConvert processes files on its own infrastructure. Your file leaves your device, travels over the internet to a US-based server, gets converted, and sits there for up to 24 hours before deletion. For most files — a meme image, a podcast clip, a product brochure — this is entirely fine. For a tax return, a medical scan, an NDA, unreleased creative work, or personal photos, the question of whether you want your file on a third-party server is worth asking explicitly.

The privacy difference, explained plainly

FreeConvert's privacy policy is reasonable — they use HTTPS, they commit to deleting files within 24 hours, and they state they don't share your files with third parties. For most use cases, that's perfectly adequate.

But consider what "uploaded to a US-based server and deleted after 24 hours" actually means for certain types of files:

  • A scanned medical report you want to compress before emailing your doctor
  • A PDF contract with salary details you're converting to share selectively
  • Personal photos from a family event
  • A spreadsheet with your company's financial data
  • An ID document you're converting to a different format

For each of these, you're making a trust decision when you use a server-based converter. You're trusting that the deletion happens as promised, that the server isn't breached during that 24-hour window, and that no employee or automated system touches your file.

Desi Jugad runs the conversion logic entirely inside your browser tab using JavaScript and WebAssembly — the same open-source libraries that power desktop tools, compiled to run in the browser. Your file is never transmitted anywhere. You can verify this yourself: open Chrome DevTools, go to the Network tab, drop a file into any Desi Jugad tool, and watch: no outgoing file upload request appears. The bytes never leave your device.

That's not a policy promise. It's a technical fact.

When FreeConvert is the better choice

We'll say it plainly: FreeConvert's format library is significantly larger than ours, and for some use cases it's the right tool.

FreeConvert handles formats that are genuinely difficult to implement in a browser environment: WMA, OGG Vorbis, FLAC, and obscure audio codecs; AVI, FLV, MKV, and legacy video containers with complex codec dependencies; ODT, RTF, and older Office formats; 3GP and mobile video formats. If you regularly convert between formats in these categories, FreeConvert's 300+ format support is a genuine advantage that browser-based tools can't yet match.

For non-sensitive files — a video clip from a camera, a music file you're reformatting for a specific device, a document from an old application — FreeConvert's server-side approach also means the conversion engine can handle heavier computational work than a browser can in real time.

Frequently asked questions

Does FreeConvert upload my files?

Yes. FreeConvert is a server-based converter — your file is uploaded to their infrastructure, converted there, and then available for download. This is standard for the category. Files are stated to be deleted after 24 hours. For non-sensitive files, this is a perfectly reasonable workflow. For confidential documents, medical records, or personal photos, a browser-based converter like Desi Jugad avoids the upload entirely.

What happens to files on FreeConvert after 24 hours?

FreeConvert's policy states files are automatically deleted from their servers after 24 hours. You are relying on their systems to execute that deletion. There is no way for an end user to independently verify it. For most files this is an acceptable level of trust. For highly sensitive documents, the only way to guarantee no server storage is to use a tool that never uploads in the first place.

Is there a truly unlimited free file converter?

Yes — Desi Jugad. Because all processing happens in your browser, there is no server to meter usage against. There is no daily cap, no file size limit (beyond your device's available RAM), no sign-up, and no cost. It's been free since launch and will remain so.

What is FreeConvert Premium and is it worth it?

FreeConvert Premium costs $9.99/month. It removes the 25-conversion/day limit and raises the file size cap from 1 GB to 5 GB. It's worth considering if you need to convert obscure formats at volume and don't mind server-side processing. If your conversions are mostly images and PDFs, Desi Jugad covers those use cases for free with no limits.

Can Desi Jugad convert video files like FreeConvert?

Yes, for common formats. Desi Jugad uses FFmpeg.wasm — a WebAssembly build of the FFmpeg library — to handle video conversion (MP4, WebM, MKV, AVI, MOV), compression, trimming, and audio extraction entirely in the browser. For very obscure legacy codecs or highly specialised video formats, FreeConvert's broader codec library may still be needed.
