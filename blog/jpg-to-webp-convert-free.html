JPG to WebP: how to reduce image file size by up to 35% free (2026)

7 min read·April 2026

Images are the single biggest drag on website performance. A typical web page has 1.5 MB of images on average, and most of those are still JPEG or PNG files that are 25–35% larger than they need to be. Converting to WebP is the single highest-impact change you can make to improve page load speed — and it takes minutes.

What makes WebP different from JPEG?

JPEG uses Discrete Cosine Transform (DCT) compression, a technique from 1992. WebP uses a more sophisticated prediction-based compression model derived from the VP8 video codec. The practical results from Google's own testing:

  • WebP lossy images are 25–34% smaller than JPEG at equivalent SSIM quality.
  • WebP lossless images are 26% smaller than PNG.
  • WebP with alpha channel is 22% smaller than equivalent 32-bit PNG.

For an e-commerce site with 500 product images averaging 200KB each (total: 100MB), switching to WebP at quality 85 would typically reduce that to ~65–75MB — a saving of 25–35MB, which translates to real money in bandwidth costs and real seconds in page load time.

Browser support in 2026

WebP is now universally supported in modern browsers. As of 2026, global browser support for WebP exceeds 97% of all users. Specifically:

  • Chrome 32+ (2014)
  • Firefox 65+ (2019)
  • Safari 14+ (2020) — this was the last major holdout
  • Edge 18+ (2018)
  • iOS Safari 14+ (2020)

The only remaining edge cases are IE11 (essentially extinct) and very old mobile browsers on Android 4.x. If your analytics show less than 0.5% of users on these, switch to WebP unconditionally. If you need to support them, use the HTML <picture> element with a WebP source and JPG fallback.

How to use <picture> for progressive WebP delivery

<picture>
  <source srcset="photo.webp" type="image/webp">
  <img src="photo.jpg" alt="Description" loading="lazy">
</picture>

Browsers that support WebP will use photo.webp. Others fall back to photo.jpg. This gives you the benefits of WebP without breaking older browsers.

How it affects Google PageSpeed / Core Web Vitals

Google's PageSpeed Insights and Lighthouse will flag JPEG and PNG images with a "Serve images in next-gen formats" recommendation. This directly affects your Largest Contentful Paint (LCP) score — one of the three Core Web Vitals that influence search ranking. Converting hero images and above-the-fold content to WebP is one of the fastest ways to improve LCP.

What quality setting should you use?

Unlike JPEG where quality 85 and quality 90 look similar but have different file sizes, WebP compression is more efficient at lower settings. Our recommendations:

  • 85–90: Product photos, hero images, portfolio — where quality is paramount.
  • 75–84: Blog images, thumbnails, inline content images.
  • 60–74: Background images, decorative images where quality is secondary.
  • Below 60: Avoid unless you're creating low-res previews or thumbnails.

WebP limitations to know

  • Not supported by Windows Photo Viewer or older image editing software — convert back to JPG if you need to edit in legacy tools.
  • Some government upload portals in India require JPG — keep your originals or convert back when needed.
  • Email clients may not display WebP inline — attach JPG for email.
