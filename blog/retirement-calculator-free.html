Retirement Calculator — Plan Your Retirement Savings in India

Published April 27, 2026

Retirement planning has one cruel property: the longer you wait to start, the exponentially harder it gets. A 25-year-old who saves ₹5,000 per month reaches the same corpus at 60 as a 35-year-old who saves ₹20,000 per month — starting 10 years earlier means needing only a quarter of the monthly investment. The math is unforgiving. Compounding rewards patience, and punishes delay more severely than almost any financial decision you will ever make.

How Much Do You Need to Retire?

The global standard is Bengen's 4% withdrawal rule: your retirement corpus should be 25 times your annual expenses. If you need ₹5 lakh per year post-retirement, you need ₹1.25 crore corpus. For India specifically, a 5–6% withdrawal rate is often used because Indian inflation historically runs at 5–6% and EPF or pension income supplements the corpus draw. A more conservative target is 30 times annual expenses, which provides meaningful buffer against longevity risk and healthcare cost spikes in later years.

Key Variables in Retirement Planning

Every retirement calculator works with the same inputs. Understanding what each one does helps you stress-test your plan rather than just accepting a single number.

  • Years to retirement: The single most powerful variable. Ten extra years of compounding at 10% roughly triples the corpus.
  • Current savings + monthly contributions: Your starting point and ongoing fuel. Even small increases compound dramatically.
  • Expected rate of return: Equity mutual funds: 10–12% historical. Balanced portfolio: 8–10%. Debt funds: 6–7%. Use conservative estimates when planning.
  • Inflation rate: Target 6% for India. This is what erodes your purchasing power between now and retirement.
  • Post-retirement life expectancy: Plan for age 85–90 as a minimum. Life expectancy is increasing. Running out of money at 82 is a real risk.
  • Post-retirement return on corpus: You will shift to lower-risk instruments after retirement. Assume 7–8% return on the corpus itself.
  • Healthcare costs: Rising dramatically with age and medical inflation in India running at 12–14% annually. Budget this separately — it is often the largest retirement expense in later years.

India-Specific Retirement Vehicles

India has several dedicated retirement savings instruments, each with distinct tax treatment, return profiles, and liquidity constraints. A good retirement plan typically uses multiple of these rather than relying on any single one.

EPF — Employees' Provident Fund

The foundation for most salaried employees. Your contribution is 12% of basic salary, matched by your employer. The current interest rate is 8.15% (FY 2023–24), and the entire maturity amount is tax-free after 5 continuous years of service. EPF is mandatory for employees earning up to ₹15,000 per month in basic salary; voluntary for those earning above that threshold. Critical mistake to avoid: withdrawing EPF when changing jobs. Each such withdrawal restarts the 5-year tax clock and permanently reduces your retirement corpus.

PPF — Public Provident Fund

Currently earning 7.1% interest (2024–25), with both the interest and the maturity amount completely tax-free. You can invest up to ₹1.5 lakh per year with a 15-year lock-in that is extendable in 5-year blocks. PPF is ideal for the fixed-income portion of a retirement portfolio — guaranteed, government-backed, and tax-efficient. The ₹1.5 lakh annual cap means it cannot be the sole retirement vehicle for anyone targeting a meaningful corpus.

NPS — National Pension System

A market-linked scheme with equity funds that have historically returned 10–12% for the Tier I account. On retirement, 40% of the corpus must be annuitized (converted to a monthly pension), while 60% can be withdrawn as a lump sum — completely tax-free. NPS also provides an additional ₹50,000 tax deduction under Section 80CCD(1B), over and above the ₹1.5 lakh 80C limit. This makes the effective post-tax return particularly attractive for those in the 30% bracket.

ELSS + SIP in Equity Mutual Funds

Over a 20–30 year horizon, equity mutual funds through systematic investment plans (SIPs) have historically returned 12–15% CAGR. After the mandatory 3-year lock-in for ELSS, there is no lock-in requirement, giving far more flexibility than EPF or PPF. For high earners who have already maximised the 80C bucket, equity SIPs in index funds and flexicap schemes are the primary engine of corpus growth.

The Retirement Corpus Calculation: Step by Step

Here is how to arrive at your personal retirement number using a structured approach:

  1. Step 1: Estimate monthly expenses at retirement in today's money. Be honest — account for travel, healthcare, and lifestyle.
  2. Step 2: Adjust for inflation to your retirement date: monthly expenses × (1.06)^years to retirement.
  3. Step 3: Calculate corpus needed: (inflation-adjusted annual expenses) ÷ withdrawal rate (4–5%).
  4. Step 4: Calculate required monthly SIP to reach that corpus using the compound interest formula, working backwards from your retirement date.

Worked example: You need ₹50,000 per month today. You plan to retire in 25 years. Inflation-adjusted monthly need at age 60: ₹50,000 × (1.06)^25 = approximately ₹2.15 lakh per month. Annual need: ₹25.8 lakh. At a 4% withdrawal rate, the required corpus is ₹6.45 crore. At 12% CAGR, reaching ₹6.45 crore in 25 years requires a monthly SIP of approximately ₹43,000 — achievable for a mid-career professional who starts immediately.

Common Retirement Planning Mistakes

  • Starting too late: Every decade of delay roughly requires tripling your monthly contribution to reach the same corpus.
  • Under-estimating inflation and healthcare: Most people budget for living expenses but forget that medical costs can become the dominant expense after age 70.
  • Relying entirely on EPF or pension: EPF alone rarely generates enough corpus for a 25-year retirement. It must be supplemented.
  • Withdrawing EPF when switching jobs: This is one of the most damaging financial habits in India. The short-term relief costs lakhs in long-term compounding.
  • Stopping investments during market downturns: Market corrections are exactly when SIPs accumulate more units at lower prices — stopping during downturns is the worst possible response.
  • Not planning for post-retirement inflation: A fixed monthly withdrawal that works at 60 will feel tight at 70 and inadequate at 80. Your corpus withdrawal strategy must account for rising expenses over time.

Frequently Asked Questions

At what age should I start planning for retirement?

The ideal answer is: your first day of employment. The practical answer is: as soon as you read this. The mathematics of compounding means that money invested at 25 is worth approximately 4–5 times more at 60 than money invested at 35, assuming a 10–12% annual return. If you're in your 30s or 40s and haven't started, don't let regret paralyse you — start now with whatever you can. The second-best time is always today.

Is EPF alone sufficient for retirement?

For most salaried employees in India, EPF alone is insufficient for a comfortable retirement. EPF contributions are capped by salary level, the interest rate (currently 8.15%) is below what equity markets have historically delivered, and the corpus typically falls short of what is needed for 25–30 years of post-retirement living expenses adjusted for inflation. EPF should be treated as the debt/fixed-income floor of a retirement portfolio, supplemented by NPS, PPF, and equity mutual fund SIPs.

What is the 4% rule and does it work in India?

The 4% rule (Bengen's rule) states that you can withdraw 4% of your corpus annually and the corpus will last at least 30 years, assuming a balanced portfolio. It was derived from US market data. In India, where inflation has historically been higher (5–7% vs 2–3% in the US), many planners use a 3.5–4% withdrawal rate for conservative planning, or supplement corpus withdrawals with income from interest-bearing instruments. The rule is a useful starting framework but should be stress-tested against your specific expense pattern and investment mix.
