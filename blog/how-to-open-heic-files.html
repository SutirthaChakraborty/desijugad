Why your HEIC photos won't open — and what to do about it

10 min read · Updated April 2026

You emailed your mum a photo from your iPhone. It arrived as a file called IMG_4421.HEIC, and she can't open it. You attached a wedding picture to a Word document on your work PC. It shows up as a broken icon. You tried to upload a photo to an older forum or a government portal — rejected.

You are running into the HEIC compatibility gap, and you are not alone. Millions of iPhone users discover this exact problem every month. The good news: it takes about five seconds to fix, permanently, without uploading a single photo to a stranger's server.

What HEIC actually is

HEIC stands for High Efficiency Image Container. It's a subset of HEIF (High Efficiency Image Format), which is itself part of the MPEG-H standard. Apple adopted HEIC as the default photo format on iPhones starting with iOS 11 in 2017.

The reason Apple switched is genuinely good: HEIC photos are typically 30 to 50 percent smaller than JPG at the same visual quality. A 4MB JPG becomes a 2MB HEIC. Over a year of heavy photography, that saves gigabytes of iCloud storage and keeps backups fast.

The reason everyone else hates it is also simple: HEIC isn't universally supported. The format requires a licensed decoder, and many platforms — including some versions of Windows, most email clients, a lot of web-based services, and every device more than a few years old — simply don't ship with one.

Where HEIC fails to open

In practice, these are the environments that still trip over HEIC files in 2026:

  • Older Windows installations — Windows 10 can handle HEIC, but only if you've installed Microsoft's HEIF Image Extensions and the HEVC Video Extensions (which costs a small fee for non-OEM users).
  • WhatsApp on older Android phones — usually works on modern phones, fails on older ones or when forwarding across platforms.
  • Most email clients — Outlook, Thunderbird, and many webmail systems show the attachment but can't preview it inline.
  • Adobe CS6 and earlier — will refuse to open the file. Adobe CC handles HEIC natively.
  • Government portals and older corporate systems — often reject anything but JPG or PNG.
  • Forum and CMS uploaders — unless the site was updated recently, HEIC uploads silently fail.

The fix for all of these is the same: convert to JPG (or occasionally PNG) before sharing.

The easy way: convert before sending

You have three realistic options:

Option 1: Change your iPhone's default

Open Settings → Camera → Formats and switch from "High Efficiency" to "Most Compatible". Every new photo will be saved as a JPG. You lose the storage savings, but you never think about compatibility again.

Downside: your existing HEIC library stays HEIC. This only helps going forward.

Option 2: AirDrop with auto-convert

If you AirDrop from an iPhone to a Mac, macOS will usually offer to convert to JPG on the fly. This is fine for one-off transfers but falls apart when you need to batch-process hundreds of photos or send them to a non-Apple device.

What about quality?

Converting HEIC to JPG does involve a tiny quality tradeoff because both formats are lossy. In practice, at quality settings of 0.85 and above, the difference is invisible to the human eye. JPG has had decades of tuning for human perceptual quality — it's a very mature format.

If you absolutely cannot afford any quality loss — say you're archiving originals for professional editing — convert to PNG instead. PNG is lossless, so the output is pixel-identical to the decoded HEIC. The tradeoff is that PNG files are often 3 to 5 times larger than either HEIC or JPG.

What about metadata and GPS location?

HEIC files carry the same EXIF metadata as JPG does — camera model, exposure settings, timestamp, and often GPS coordinates. When you convert, most of this metadata is preserved in the JPG output, with one important exception: GPS coordinates are often stripped by default on privacy-first converters.

That's usually what you want. If you're posting to social media, emailing a real estate photo, or sending pictures to a forum, you almost certainly don't want to leak the exact latitude and longitude where you took the photo. If you do need GPS preserved (say, for a geotagged travel album), use a converter that explicitly keeps EXIF intact.

A note on "online HEIC converter" search results

Most of the top results for "HEIC to JPG" upload your photos to a server, do the conversion remotely, and give you a link back. This is the legacy model, and it has three real problems:

  • Your photos exist on someone else's disk, at least temporarily. Terms of service vary wildly in what they promise about retention.
  • Free tiers often have file size limits because server bandwidth costs money. HEIC files from modern iPhones can be 3–8MB each, and batches hit those limits fast.
  • The entire business model depends on displaying ads to you, which means the page is often bloated and slow.

Browser-based converters solve all three at once. There's no server, so there's no upload, no bandwidth bottleneck, and no need for aggressive advertising. The tradeoff is purely one of discoverability — most people haven't realized this approach exists, because Google rewards old domains with established authority.

Bottom line

HEIC isn't going anywhere — it's too useful a format for iPhones and too deeply embedded in the Apple ecosystem. Compatibility will improve slowly over the next few years, but for now, expect to convert before sharing outside the Apple garden.

If you do it often, bookmark a private browser-based converter, change your iPhone's default to "Most Compatible" for new photos you know you'll share, and stop thinking about it.
