How to remove EXIF metadata from photos before sharing (2026)

9 min read·April 2026

Every photo taken on a modern smartphone contains far more than the visible image. Embedded invisibly in the file is a block of data called EXIF (Exchangeable Image File Format) metadata — and it can reveal your exact GPS location, the device you used, the date and time, and more. When you share a photo online, that data comes with it — unless you strip it first.

What EXIF data actually contains

Open any photo taken on an iPhone or Android phone in an EXIF viewer and you'll typically find:

  • GPS coordinates — latitude and longitude accurate to within 10 metres, sometimes even an altitude reading.
  • Device make and model — "Apple iPhone 15 Pro" or "Samsung Galaxy S24 Ultra".
  • Camera settings — aperture (f/1.8), shutter speed (1/120s), ISO, focal length, flash status.
  • Date and time — when the photo was taken, down to the second.
  • Software — the iOS/Android version or editing app used.
  • Orientation — which way the phone was held.
  • Copyright and artist fields — if set by a camera or editing software.

The GPS field is the most sensitive. A photo taken at home reveals your home address. A photo taken at work reveals your workplace. A selfie taken at a hospital reveals a medical visit. This isn't theoretical — there are well-documented cases of stalkers locating victims through EXIF GPS data in photos posted online.

Does social media strip EXIF data?

Most major platforms do strip EXIF when you upload:

  • Instagram — strips most EXIF including GPS ✅
  • Facebook/WhatsApp — strips EXIF ✅
  • Twitter/X — strips EXIF ✅
  • Reddit — strips EXIF ✅
  • Email attachments — does NOT strip EXIF ❌
  • Google Drive / Dropbox shared links — does NOT strip EXIF ❌
  • Direct file sharing (AirDrop, USB, Telegram) — does NOT strip EXIF ❌
  • Matrimonial / job application portals — usually does NOT strip EXIF ❌

The risk isn't social media — it's email attachments, shared Google Drive folders, matrimonial profile photos, forum uploads, and direct messaging apps. These preserve the full file with all metadata intact.

Real privacy risks from EXIF GPS data

Some concrete scenarios where unstripped EXIF data causes harm:

  1. Home address exposure: Posting a product for sale on OLX or Facebook Marketplace with a photo taken at home exposes your exact home coordinates to every potential buyer.
  2. Domestic abuse: Survivors who have escaped abusive situations can be tracked down through a single photo shared to any platform that doesn't strip EXIF.
  3. Targeted phishing: Knowing your device model helps attackers craft more convincing device-specific phishing messages.
  4. Corporate espionage: Photos taken inside an office and shared externally can reveal the building's location, the date/time of sensitive meetings, and device inventory.

The most privacy-safe method is the browser Canvas approach: the image is decoded and re-drawn through a Canvas element, which discards all metadata — only the raw pixel data remains.

Other methods to strip EXIF

  • Windows: Right-click image → Properties → Details → "Remove Properties and Personal Information" at the bottom.
  • Mac: Preview does not strip EXIF on its own. Use Image Capture or a third-party tool.
  • iPhone: Photos app → Share → "Options" (at the top of the share sheet) → disable "Location".
  • Android: Google Photos → Share → "Remove location data" option before sharing.
  • ExifTool (CLI): exiftool -all= photo.jpg — removes all metadata. Powerful batch tool.

Does stripping EXIF affect image quality?

The canvas re-draw method involves a tiny quality reduction on JPEG (re-encoding at 95% quality). If you choose PNG output, there's no quality loss at all — PNG is lossless. For most practical purposes, a 95%-quality JPEG is indistinguishable from the original. If you're a professional photographer concerned about quality loss, use ExifTool directly — it strips metadata without any re-encoding.
