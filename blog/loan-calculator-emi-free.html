Loan Calculator — Calculate EMI Monthly Payment Free in India

Published April 27, 2026

An EMI (Equated Monthly Instalment) is the fixed monthly payment you make to repay a loan. The word "equated" means equal — every month, you pay the exact same total amount. Inside that fixed payment, however, the split between interest and principal shifts dramatically over time: you pay mostly interest in the early months and mostly principal in the later months. Understanding how EMI works helps you evaluate loan offers, decide on the right tenure, and plan prepayments at the most effective time.

The EMI Formula

EMI = P × r × (1 + r)^n / ((1 + r)^n − 1)

Where P = principal loan amount, r = monthly interest rate (annual rate ÷ 12 ÷ 100), n = total number of monthly instalments.

Example: ₹10 lakh home loan at 8.5% for 20 years. r = 8.5 ÷ 12 ÷ 100 = 0.00708. n = 240. EMI ≈ ₹8,678. Total amount paid = ₹8,678 × 240 = ₹20.83 lakh. Total interest paid = ₹10.83 lakh — more than the original loan amount. This is the sobering reality of long-tenure loans that most borrowers never fully calculate.

Principal vs Interest in Each EMI

The amortisation schedule reveals how your EMI is split each month. In the example above (₹10 lakh at 8.5%, 20 years):

  • Month 1 interest component: ₹10,00,000 × 0.00708 = ₹7,083
  • Month 1 principal component: ₹8,678 − ₹7,083 = ₹1,595

In the very first month, 82% of your EMI goes to interest and only 18% reduces your debt. As the months pass and your outstanding principal decreases, the interest component shrinks and the principal component grows. By the final few months, nearly your entire EMI is principal repayment. This structure is called a "reducing balance" loan — and it is the reason why prepayments made in the early years of a loan are so much more powerful than prepayments made near the end.

Impact of Tenure and Interest Rate

The same ₹10 lakh loan at 8.5% interest — but different tenures — tells a strikingly different story:

Impact of Prepayment

Prepayment is one of the most effective personal finance decisions available to loan borrowers — especially when made early. Making a ₹1 lakh prepayment in the 3rd year of the 20-year ₹10 lakh loan at 8.5% saves approximately ₹2.7 lakh in interest and cuts the tenure by about 2.5 years. That is a 2.7× return on the prepaid amount — effectively guaranteed and risk-free.

The timing matters enormously because of the amortisation structure: early in the loan, a large fraction of your outstanding balance is "future interest" — and prepaying principal eliminates that future interest directly. The same ₹1 lakh prepaid in year 18 (near the end) saves far less because most of the interest was already paid in earlier years. Even a modest extra payment of ₹10,000 per month over the standard EMI can save substantial interest and cut several years off a 20-year home loan tenure.

Comparing Loan Offers — Effective Interest Rate

Not all loan interest rates are quoted the same way. Some lenders — especially for personal and consumer loans — quote a flat interest rate. This sounds lower but is actually much more expensive than a reducing balance rate.

Example: ₹10 lakh personal loan at 10% flat rate for 5 years. Monthly interest = ₹10 lakh × 10% ÷ 12 = ₹8,333 (always the same, on original principal). Monthly principal repayment = ₹10 lakh ÷ 60 = ₹16,667. Total EMI = ₹25,000. Total interest = ₹8,333 × 60 = ₹5 lakh. The effective reducing balance rate on this loan is approximately 18% — nearly double the stated 10% flat rate.

Always ask lenders whether the rate is flat or reducing balance. Compare EMIs and total interest payable, not just the headline rate. Banks and NBFCs regulated by RBI are required to disclose the annualised rate — insist on it.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is it better to increase EMI or make lump sum prepayment?

Both reduce your total interest outgo, but in different ways. Increasing your monthly EMI reduces the loan tenure faster and works automatically every month — ideal if you get a salary increment. Lump sum prepayment (using a bonus or inheritance) reduces your outstanding principal immediately and dramatically lowers future interest. If your loan has a prepayment penalty (common with fixed-rate loans), check the penalty amount versus the interest savings first. For most floating-rate home loans in India, there is no prepayment penalty for individual borrowers — so prepaying whenever you have surplus funds is almost always a good decision.

What happens if I miss an EMI payment?

Missing an EMI typically triggers a late payment penalty (usually 1–2% of the EMI per month) and adds a negative entry to your CIBIL credit report. Even one missed EMI can lower your credit score by 50–100 points, making future loans more expensive or harder to obtain. If you anticipate difficulty, contact your lender proactively to request a moratorium (temporary pause) rather than simply missing the payment — most banks offer structured relief and it protects your credit score. A formal moratorium is always better than a default on your record.

Can I get a lower EMI by refinancing my loan?

Yes — loan refinancing (balance transfer to another lender at a lower interest rate) is a common and legal strategy. If your current home loan is at 9.5% and another bank offers 8.5%, refinancing saves 1% per year on the outstanding balance. On ₹40 lakh outstanding, that is ₹40,000/year in interest savings. The costs of refinancing include processing fees (0.5–1% of loan amount), legal fees, and stamp duty in some states. Calculate the break-even point: if the refinancing saves ₹40,000/year and costs ₹50,000 in fees, you break even in about 15 months. For a loan with 10+ years remaining, refinancing almost always pays off.
