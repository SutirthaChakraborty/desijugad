Word counter online free — count words, characters, sentences (2026)

6 min read·April 2026

Word count is one of those deceptively simple metrics that matters in dozens of contexts: blog post length for SEO, essay word limits for academia, character limits for social media, reading time estimates for content marketing, and bid limits for freelance writing proposals. A good word counter does more than count words — it gives you the full statistical profile of your text.

What our word counter tracks

  • Word count: Total words. Multiple spaces are treated as single delimiters.
  • Character count (with spaces): Total characters including spaces.
  • Character count (without spaces): Characters only — relevant for Twitter, SMS, and some publishing limits.
  • Sentence count: Segments ended by period, exclamation, or question mark.
  • Paragraph count: Double line breaks as paragraph separators.
  • Reading time: Based on 200–250 words per minute (average adult reading speed).
  • Keyword density: Most frequent words and their occurrence count — useful for SEO.

Word count guidelines for SEO (blog posts)

SEO word count is debated, but generally:

  • 300–600 words: Thin content. Unlikely to rank for competitive keywords.
  • 600–1,000 words: Minimum for basic informational posts.
  • 1,000–2,000 words: Sweet spot for most blog posts. Covers the topic thoroughly without padding.
  • 2,000–4,000 words: Long-form content. Tends to rank well for complex topics, acquire more backlinks.
  • 4,000+ words: Pillar content, guides, and comprehensive resources. High effort but strong ranking potential.

More important than length: comprehensive coverage of the topic. A 2,000-word post that fully answers the search intent beats a 4,000-word post that pads with irrelevant content.

Word count targets by content type

Different writing contexts have very different word count norms. Here are the specific numbers that matter across the most common use cases:

Social media:

  • Twitter/X: 280 characters hard limit (links count as 23 characters)
  • LinkedIn post: 150–300 words for best engagement; 3,000 character hard limit
  • Instagram caption: 138–150 words optimal (only ~125 characters shown before "more"); 2,200 character hard limit
  • Facebook post: 40–80 words is the optimal range for engagement; longer posts see sharp drop-off in reach

Blog posts and web content:

  • 300+ words: minimum for Google to consider indexing a page as meaningful content
  • 1,000–1,500 words: solid informational post; covers a topic thoroughly for most search intents
  • 2,000+ words: appropriate for competitive keywords where top-ranking pages are comprehensive
  • 3,000+ words: pillar content, cornerstone articles, definitive guides; high effort but strongest ranking potential

Email:

  • Subject line: 6–8 words (40–50 characters) for best open rates
  • Transactional email body: 50–125 words — concise, single action
  • Newsletter: 200–300 words with a clear CTA; longer newsletters have sharply lower read rates

Academic writing:

  • Undergraduate essay: 1,500–3,000 words (varies by assignment and institution)
  • Master's thesis: 15,000–45,000 words depending on discipline
  • PhD thesis: 80,000–100,000 words is the standard range in most UK/US universities

Professional documents:

  • Resume/CV: 400–600 words for most roles; one page for under 10 years experience
  • Cover letter: 250–400 words; three to four short paragraphs
  • Executive summary: equivalent to 1–2 pages (250–500 words); distills the key points of a longer document

Reading time calculator

The average adult reads at 200–250 words per minute for standard prose. This varies by content type: dense technical material is typically read at 150–200 wpm, while light narrative content can be read at 250–300 wpm. The 200–250 wpm range is the standard used by most reading time estimators, including Medium.

Practical reading time benchmarks:

  • 500 words ≈ 2 minutes
  • 1,000 words ≈ 4 minutes
  • 1,500 words ≈ 6 minutes
  • 2,500 words ≈ 10 minutes
  • 5,000 words ≈ 20 minutes

Reading time estimates displayed in article metadata ("8 min read") have a measurable effect on click-through rates from search results and social shares. Users self-select based on available time — a person with 3 minutes on their commute will click a "3 min read" over a "12 min read" for the same topic. Blog posts that include reading time estimates in their meta description or page header tend to see higher engagement because they set accurate expectations before the user commits to clicking.

Why word count is not the only metric

Word count is a useful proxy, not a direct ranking signal. Google has stated repeatedly that there is no minimum word count for ranking. Long content tends to perform well in search because of what length correlates with — not because of the word count itself.

Long content tends to:

  • Cover more subtopics and related questions, satisfying a broader range of search intents
  • Attract more backlinks from other sites that find it useful as a reference
  • Generate more time-on-page and deeper scroll depth, both of which are positive user engagement signals
  • Include more naturally occurring keyword variations and related terms (LSI keywords)

But these benefits only materialise if the content is genuinely good. 500 exceptional words that fully answer a user's question will outperform 3,000 padded words that circle the topic without landing anywhere. Readability (measured by Flesch-Kincaid score or similar), time-on-page, bounce rate, and click-through rate from search are all equally important signals. Use word count as a starting benchmark — "am I covering this topic with enough depth?" — not as a target to hit by adding filler sentences.

Frequently asked questions

How many words is a 5-minute read?

At the average adult reading speed of 200–250 words per minute, a 5-minute read is approximately 1,000–1,250 words. For denser technical content where readers slow down, 750–900 words may take 5 minutes. For light, fast-paced writing, you might read 1,200–1,400 words in 5 minutes. Most reading time estimators use 200 wpm as their baseline for a conservative estimate, which means 1,000 words = 5 minutes is the most widely cited benchmark.

Does word count affect SEO?

Word count is not a direct Google ranking factor — Google has confirmed this. However, longer content tends to rank better for competitive keywords because it is more likely to cover a topic comprehensively, attract backlinks, generate higher time-on-page, and satisfy multiple related search intents in a single piece. A 2,000-word article that thoroughly covers a topic will typically outrank a 500-word stub, but this is because of the comprehensiveness, not the word count itself. 500 genuinely useful words beat 3,000 padded words every time. Focus on covering the topic completely, then check if your word count is in range for what top-ranking pages are doing.

What is the ideal blog post length for ranking?

It depends on the keyword and competition. For low-competition informational queries, 800–1,200 words is often sufficient. For competitive keywords where the top results are comprehensive guides, 1,500–3,000 words is typically necessary to compete. The most reliable approach: search for your target keyword, look at the top 3–5 ranking pages, and assess their depth and length.
