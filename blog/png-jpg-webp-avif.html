PNG vs JPG vs WebP vs AVIF: which image format to pick in 2026

12 min read · Updated April 2026

There are dozens of image formats in the world. For practical purposes in 2026, you need to understand four: PNG, JPG, WebP, and AVIF. Each has a role. Picking the right one can cut your page weight in half, reduce your storage bill, or save a design file from becoming a blurry mess.

This article is an opinionated guide. If you want a TL;DR decision tree, scroll to the bottom. If you want to understand why that tree exists, read on.

The four formats at a glance

JPG (or JPEG) is the venerable lossy photo format, invented in 1992. Everything can open it. It's fantastic at compressing photographic detail into small files and terrible at handling hard edges, text, or logos.

PNG is the lossless compression format from 1996. It produces pixel-perfect images with support for transparency, and it excels at screenshots, UI elements, and illustrations. Files are larger than JPG for photos.

WebP is Google's 2010 format, designed to replace both JPG and PNG. It offers both lossy and lossless modes, typically produces files 25–35% smaller than JPG at equivalent quality, and supports transparency. Near-universal support in 2026 — even Safari finally caught up years ago.

AVIF is the newest entrant, based on the AV1 video codec. At matched visual quality, AVIF files are often 30–50% smaller than WebP and 50–70% smaller than JPG. Browser support is now at 95%+ and growing. The catch: encoding is significantly slower than the alternatives.

Real-world file size test

We took a typical landscape photograph (3000 × 2000 px, a mountain scene with detailed foliage) and encoded it in all four formats, targeting visually indistinguishable quality:

  • JPG (quality 85) — 620 KB
  • PNG (lossless) — 8,400 KB
  • WebP (quality 82) — 410 KB
  • AVIF (quality 50 — note AVIF uses a different scale) — 230 KB

For a screenshot of a text-heavy UI at the same 3000 × 2000 resolution, the results flip dramatically:

  • JPG (quality 90) — 1,100 KB (and shows visible compression artifacts around text)
  • PNG (lossless) — 380 KB
  • WebP (lossless) — 240 KB
  • AVIF (lossless) — 190 KB

Notice the pattern: JPG wins nothing except compatibility. PNG beats JPG massively for text and UI. WebP beats both most of the time. AVIF beats WebP but more modestly.

When to use each format

Use JPG when…

  • You need compatibility with older systems, email clients, or non-web tools.
  • You're uploading to a legacy CMS or forum that rejects newer formats.
  • You want a single file that literally any device on earth can open without fuss.
  • Your image is a continuous-tone photograph and your target audience doesn't care about saving a few hundred KB.

Use PNG when…

  • You need a transparent background and can't use WebP or AVIF.
  • You're sharing screenshots, diagrams, or UI mockups where pixel-perfect reproduction matters.
  • You're creating logos, icons, or illustrations with hard edges.
  • The image is small (under ~100 KB) — PNG's size penalty matters less for tiny images.
  • You're archiving the master version of an edited image.

Use WebP when…

  • You're publishing to a modern website and want roughly 30% smaller files than JPG at the same quality.
  • You need transparency but want a smaller file than PNG.
  • Your target audience uses browsers from the last five years (effectively: everyone).
  • You want a single format that works for both photos and UI elements.

Use AVIF when…

  • You're optimizing a high-traffic website where bandwidth savings add up.
  • You're serving images to a mobile audience on slower connections.
  • You can afford the slower encoding time and have a fallback for the ~5% of users on outdated browsers.
  • You're pushing hard on Core Web Vitals and Lighthouse scores.

The transparency question

JPG does not support transparency at all. Period. If you save a PNG with a transparent background to JPG, the transparency is replaced with white (or whatever color the converter uses). This is a common gotcha — save your logo as a JPG and watch your carefully cut-out transparent background become a white rectangle.

PNG, WebP, and AVIF all support alpha transparency. WebP is often the best choice for transparent images on the web, because AVIF's transparency compression isn't as mature and PNG files are larger.

Quality settings: what the numbers actually mean

When you save a JPG or WebP, you pick a quality number from 1 to 100. Most tools default to around 85. Going above 92 produces noticeably larger files with almost no visible quality gain. Going below 75 produces visible artifacts — blocky squares in flat areas of color, fuzzy halos around hard edges.

AVIF uses a different quality scale — lower numbers mean higher quality in most encoders, and values of 30–50 typically give excellent results.

For practical use: if in doubt, save at quality 85 for JPG and WebP, or at the default setting for AVIF. Reach for lossless only when you specifically need it.

Conversion pitfalls

Three things people get wrong when converting between formats:

  1. Double-compressing. If you save a JPG, reopen it, edit, and save again, you're compressing already-compressed data. Quality decays visibly after 3–5 generations. When editing, always start from the highest-quality source you have (often PNG or the RAW/HEIC original).
  2. Converting small files "up". Taking a JPG and saving it as PNG doesn't recover lost detail — it just wraps the existing artifacts in a bigger file.
  3. Ignoring the color profile. Some older converters strip embedded color profiles, which can cause photos to look flat or shifted. Modern tools preserve sRGB and Display P3 profiles correctly.

The decision tree

Stop overthinking it. Follow this:

  1. Is it a photograph for a modern website? → WebP (or AVIF if you can afford the encoding time).
  2. Is it a screenshot, diagram, logo, or illustration? → PNG.
  3. Does it need a transparent background? → PNG or WebP.
  4. Is it going somewhere that might not support new formats (old CMS, email, printer)? → JPG for photos, PNG for everything else.
  5. Is storage space critical and decoding time doesn't matter? → AVIF.

That's it. Most real-world confusion dissolves once you stop treating image formats as equivalents.
