Lead Value Calculator — Know Your Marketing ROI

Published April 27, 2026

A lead is only worth what you can ultimately extract from it — but most businesses have no idea what that number actually is. If you don't know your lead value, you cannot rationally decide how much to spend on advertising, how many salespeople to hire, or whether your CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost) is sustainable. Lead value is the bridge between marketing activity and business profitability. Without it, every ad budget decision is a guess dressed up as strategy.

The Lead Value Formula

Lead Value = Average Order Value × Conversion Rate

Example: If your average sale is ₹20,000 and 15% of leads become customers: Lead Value = ₹20,000 × 0.15 = ₹3,000. This means you can spend up to ₹3,000 to acquire a lead and break even. Spend less than ₹3,000 per lead and you make money. Spend more and you lose money. This single number anchors every advertising budget decision.

The lead value calculation also reveals immediately whether your current advertising is profitable. If you're paying ₹4,000 per lead through Google Ads but your lead value is ₹3,000, you are paying ₹1,000 to acquire every customer — a loss that will compound as you scale.

Customer Lifetime Value vs Single-Sale Lead Value

The basic formula above assumes a single purchase. For businesses with repeat customers, the calculation extends across the full relationship:

CLV = Average Order Value × Purchase Frequency × Average Customer Lifespan

Example: ₹2,000 average order, customer buys 4 times per year, stays a customer for 3 years = ₹24,000 CLV. Lead Value using CLV = ₹24,000 × 0.15 = ₹3,600. This is 20% higher than the single-sale calculation, meaning you can afford 20% more in customer acquisition cost while remaining profitable.

For subscription businesses, the CLV formula becomes: CLV = Monthly Recurring Revenue ÷ Monthly Churn Rate. At 2% monthly churn, the average customer stays 50 months. If they pay ₹500 per month, CLV = ₹25,000. This is why subscription companies routinely spend heavily on acquisition — they are buying long revenue streams, not single transactions.

The LTV to CAC Ratio

The LTV:CAC ratio is the most important single metric for any growth-stage business. It tells you how much value you extract from a customer relative to what you spent to acquire them.

  • LTV = ₹30,000, CAC = ₹10,000 (3:1 ratio): Sustainable, investable business. VCs typically want to see at least 3:1 before backing marketing scale-up.
  • LTV = ₹15,000, CAC = ₹10,000 (1.5:1 ratio): Burning money. Every new customer acquired makes the business poorer on a unit economics basis. Scaling marketing here destroys value.
  • LTV = ₹30,000, CAC = ₹3,000 (10:1 ratio): Underinvesting in growth. There is significant opportunity to spend more on acquisition, capture market share, and still be highly profitable.

A ratio below 1:1 is existential — the business cannot survive at scale. A ratio between 1:1 and 2:1 requires immediate fixes to unit economics. Above 3:1 is the growth zone. Above 5:1 often indicates underinvestment in marketing.

MQL, SQL, and the Lead Pipeline

Not all leads are equal. Breaking down your pipeline by lead quality stage gives you far more diagnostic power than a single conversion rate.

  • Lead: Any contact who shows interest — fills out a form, calls your number, downloads a resource.
  • Marketing Qualified Lead (MQL): A lead that meets criteria suggesting they are a plausible customer — right industry, company size, job title, or demonstrated behaviour pattern.
  • Sales Qualified Lead (SQL): An MQL that the sales team has vetted as ready and willing to have a purchase conversation.

Pipeline example: 1,000 leads → 200 MQLs (20% lead-to-MQL) → 50 SQLs (25% MQL-to-SQL) → 15 customers (30% SQL-to-customer). Overall lead-to-customer rate: 1.5%. This breakdown shows exactly where to fix the funnel. If lead-to-MQL is only 5%, your advertising is reaching the wrong audience. If MQL-to-SQL is 10%, your qualification criteria are too loose. Each stage has a different lever and a different owner.

Improving Lead Value: The Three Levers

There are only three ways to improve lead value, and they are not equal in effort or impact:

  1. Increase average order value: Upsell to premium tiers, bundle products, create packages. A 20% increase in average order value increases lead value by 20% — without changing any marketing.
  2. Increase conversion rate: Better lead qualification, faster follow-up (research consistently shows contacting a lead within 1 hour increases conversion dramatically), improved CRM processes, sales training. Doubling your conversion rate from 10% to 20% doubles your lead value — without spending an additional rupee on lead generation. This is often the highest-ROI improvement available.
  3. Increase customer lifetime: Retention programs, product improvement, proactive customer success, loyalty incentives. Extending the average customer lifespan from 2 years to 3 years increases CLV by 50% and has a direct multiplier effect on every lead value calculation.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a good lead-to-customer conversion rate?

Industry benchmarks vary significantly by sector. B2B SaaS typically converts 1–5% of leads to customers. E-commerce sees 1–3% visitor-to-purchase conversion. Real estate runs 0.4–1.2% due to high-consideration purchase cycles. Inside sales of qualified B2B leads can reach 15–25% close rates. The most important comparison is not against an industry average but against your own historical performance — and against the unit economics of whether your current conversion rate, combined with your CAC and AOV, yields a profitable LTV:CAC ratio.

How do I calculate ROI on marketing campaigns using lead value?

Campaign ROI = ((Revenue Generated - Campaign Cost) / Campaign Cost) × 100. To attribute revenue to a specific campaign, multiply the number of leads generated by your lead value: if a Facebook campaign generated 200 leads and your lead value is ₹2,500, the campaign's expected revenue contribution is ₹5 lakh. If the campaign cost ₹80,000, the ROI is ((₹5,00,000 - ₹80,000) / ₹80,000) × 100 = 525%. Track this across channels to identify where to allocate more budget and where to cut.

What is the difference between a lead and a prospect?

A lead is anyone who has expressed any level of interest — they filled out a form, called your number, or engaged with your content. A prospect is a lead who has been evaluated and determined to have a genuine fit with your product or service and the budget and authority to make a purchase. In pipeline terminology, a prospect is roughly equivalent to a Sales Qualified Lead (SQL). The distinction matters because the time your sales team spends on low-quality leads is directly subtracted from time available for qualified prospects — proper qualification at the MQL stage is therefore a revenue-generating activity, not just an administrative filter.
