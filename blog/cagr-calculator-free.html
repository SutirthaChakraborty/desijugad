CAGR Calculator — Measure Investment Returns Accurately

Published April 27, 2026

CAGR — Compound Annual Growth Rate — is the single most important metric for comparing investments, business growth, and financial targets. Unlike simple year-on-year growth rates that get distorted by volatile years, CAGR smooths out all the ups and downs to show what constant annual growth would have produced the same end result. One number captures years of fluctuation and makes apples-to-apples comparison possible.

The CAGR Formula

CAGR = (Ending Value / Beginning Value)^(1/n) − 1

Where n = number of years. The result is expressed as a decimal; multiply by 100 for a percentage.

Example: ₹1 lakh invested grows to ₹2.5 lakh in 7 years. CAGR = (2.5 / 1)^(1/7) − 1 = 2.5^0.1429 − 1 = 1.1399 − 1 = 13.99% ≈ 14%. That single number — 14% — tells you precisely how fast your money compounded, regardless of whether year 3 was −20% and year 5 was +40%.

CAGR vs Absolute Return vs XIRR

These three metrics are frequently confused, but each answers a different question:

  • Absolute Return = (Final Value − Initial Value) / Initial Value × 100. Tells you the total percentage gain but says nothing about how long it took. A 100% return sounds great — but not if it took 20 years.
  • CAGR = annualises the return so you can compare investments held for different durations. A 100% return in 5 years is 14.9% CAGR. A 100% return in 10 years is only 7.2% CAGR — very different stories.
  • XIRR = the actual annualised return when cash flows are irregular — multiple investments made at different times, like SIP instalments. CAGR assumes one lump sum in and one lump sum out; XIRR handles the real world.

When to use each: Use CAGR when comparing two investments with different holding periods. Use XIRR for your mutual fund SIP portfolio or any investment where you added/withdrew money at irregular intervals. Use Absolute Return for a quick "how much did I make in total" sanity check.

Rule of 72 — Quick Mental Math

The Rule of 72 is a mental shortcut: divide 72 by the CAGR to find approximately how many years it takes to double your money.

  • 12% CAGR: doubles in 72 ÷ 12 = 6 years
  • 8% CAGR: doubles in 72 ÷ 8 = 9 years
  • 6% CAGR: doubles in 72 ÷ 6 = 12 years
  • 4% CAGR (savings account): doubles in 72 ÷ 4 = 18 years

You can also reverse it: to double your money in 5 years, you need 72 ÷ 5 = 14.4% CAGR. This is why most conservative investments fail to build real wealth — their CAGR is too low to double money within a planning horizon that matters.

Limitations of CAGR

CAGR is powerful but imperfect. Knowing its limitations makes you a smarter investor. First, CAGR completely masks volatility. A portfolio that went +50%, −30%, +50%, −30%, +50% and a portfolio with a steady 10% every year can have the same CAGR — but the experience and risk are completely different. Second, CAGR assumes a straight-line path from start to end. The interim route (drawdowns, recoveries) is invisible. Third, CAGR is not suitable for portfolios where cash was added or withdrawn — use XIRR instead. Fourth, comparing investments with mismatched start or end dates using CAGR can be misleading if market conditions at those dates were unusually high or low.

Despite these limitations, CAGR remains the best single-number summary of investment performance and the standard metric used by mutual fund fact sheets, financial advisors, and SEBI-regulated disclosures in India.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a good CAGR for mutual funds in India?

A mutual fund's CAGR should be evaluated against its benchmark index. For large-cap equity funds, a CAGR above the Nifty 50's historical 13–14% over a 10-year period indicates genuine alpha generation. For mid and small-cap funds, the bar is higher — 15–18% CAGR is expected given the additional risk. For debt funds, a CAGR consistently 0.5–1% above equivalent FD rates indicates good management. Always compare across the same time period and against the fund's own stated benchmark, not an unrelated index.

How is CAGR different from IRR?

CAGR (Compound Annual Growth Rate) assumes a single starting investment and a single ending value — no cash flows in between. It is the simplest annualised return metric. IRR (Internal Rate of Return) handles multiple cash flows at different points in time — it finds the discount rate that makes the net present value of all cash flows equal to zero. XIRR is the Excel/finance version of IRR for irregular date-based cash flows. For a lump sum investment held to maturity, CAGR and IRR give the same result. For SIP investments or any portfolio with additions/withdrawals, use XIRR — it is the only accurate measure.

Can CAGR be negative?

Yes. If your ending value is less than your beginning value, the CAGR is negative. For example, ₹1 lakh invested in a stock that is worth ₹70,000 after 3 years has a CAGR of (0.7)^(1/3) − 1 = −11.1%. A negative CAGR simply means the investment lost value on an annualised basis. This is common with individual stocks, sector funds, or investments made at market peaks. It is also possible for an investment to show a positive absolute return but a lower CAGR than inflation, which means it lost real purchasing power even while growing in nominal terms.
