BMI Calculator Online Free — Calculate Your Body Mass Index

April 27, 2026 • 4 min read

BMI (Body Mass Index) is one of the most widely used health screening tools worldwide, yet it is also one of the most misunderstood. Calculated from just height and weight, it is a quick indicator — not a diagnostic. Understanding what your BMI actually means, what it does not measure, and how to use it alongside other health metrics gives you a much more accurate picture of where you stand.

The BMI Formula

BMI is calculated using a straightforward mathematical formula. In metric units: BMI = weight (kg) / height (m)². In imperial units: BMI = (weight in lbs × 703) / height in inches².

Here is a worked example. A person who weighs 70 kg and stands 1.75 m tall has a BMI of: 70 ÷ (1.75 × 1.75) = 70 ÷ 3.0625 = 22.9. This falls squarely in the "Normal weight" category. The formula itself is simple — interpretation is where it gets more nuanced.

BMI Categories (WHO Standard)

BMI Range Category
Below 18.5 Underweight
18.5 – 24.9 Normal weight
25.0 – 29.9 Overweight
30.0 – 34.9 Class I Obesity
35.0 – 39.9 Class II Obesity
40 and above Class III Obesity

Important note for South and East Asian populations: WHO and many national health agencies use lower BMI thresholds for Asian adults. Overweight starts at 23, and obesity starts at 27.5 — because research consistently shows that South and East Asian populations develop metabolic conditions such as type 2 diabetes and cardiovascular disease at lower BMI values compared to European populations. If you are of South Asian or East Asian descent, you should use these adjusted thresholds when interpreting your result.

What BMI Does NOT Measure

This is the most important thing to understand about BMI: it does not distinguish between fat mass, muscle mass, and bone density. It is purely a ratio of weight to height squared.

Consider two people with a BMI of 28. The first is a competitive weightlifter with 12% body fat and substantial muscle mass — technically "overweight" by BMI, but extremely healthy. The second is a sedentary office worker with 32% body fat and minimal muscle — the same BMI, but with a very different health profile. BMI treats them identically.

BMI also ignores fat distribution entirely. Visceral fat — the fat stored around your internal organs in the abdominal cavity — is metabolically far more dangerous than subcutaneous fat stored under the skin. A person with a "normal" BMI of 23 but a large waist circumference (indicating high visceral fat) may carry greater cardiovascular risk than someone with a BMI of 27 who carries weight more evenly.

Better Health Metrics to Use Alongside BMI

BMI works best as one data point among several. These additional measures give a fuller picture:

  • Waist circumference: High risk threshold is above 88 cm (35 inches) for women and above 102 cm (40 inches) for men. Directly measures abdominal fat accumulation.
  • Waist-to-hip ratio: Divide waist measurement by hip measurement. Healthy range is below 0.85 for women and below 0.90 for men.
  • Body fat percentage: Measured accurately via DEXA scan or bioelectrical impedance. Healthy ranges differ by age and sex — typically 20–32% for women and 8–19% for men at normal weight.
  • Blood pressure, fasting glucose, and cholesterol: These direct metabolic markers predict disease risk more precisely than BMI alone. A person can have a "normal" BMI but elevated blood glucose indicating pre-diabetes.

How to Use Your BMI Result Practically

Rather than treating your BMI as a verdict, treat it as a starting question to explore with a healthcare provider.

  • BMI under 18.5 (Underweight): Consult a doctor to rule out underlying conditions such as thyroid disorders, nutrient malabsorption, or disordered eating. Focus on nutrient-dense foods and, if appropriate, resistance training to build muscle.
  • BMI 25–29.9 (Overweight): Small lifestyle changes have significant impact. Research shows that losing just 5–10% of body weight meaningfully reduces blood pressure, cholesterol, and cardiovascular risk — even if you remain technically "overweight."
  • BMI 30 and above (Obese): Medical consultation is recommended. Gradual, sustained lifestyle intervention — not crash dieting — produces the best long-term outcomes. Focus on consistent small changes rather than extreme short-term restrictions.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is BMI accurate for athletes and bodybuilders?

No — BMI is particularly unreliable for athletes and people who do significant strength training. Because muscle is denser than fat, a muscular person will have a higher weight at the same height, pushing their BMI into "overweight" or even "obese" territory despite having low body fat. For athletes, body fat percentage measured via DEXA scan is a far more meaningful metric than BMI.

Why is the BMI threshold different for Asian populations?

Large-scale studies have found that South Asian and East Asian populations develop insulin resistance, type 2 diabetes, and cardiovascular disease at lower BMI values than European populations. The mechanism is partly genetic — Asian bodies tend to accumulate more visceral (abdominal) fat at lower overall body weights. WHO and national health bodies in countries like India, China, Japan, and Singapore have adopted lower cutoffs: overweight from BMI 23, obesity from BMI 27.5.

What is a healthy BMI for my age?

The standard WHO BMI categories apply to adults aged 18 and above without age-specific adjustments, though older adults (65+) often have better health outcomes at slightly higher BMI values (23–27) because some extra weight provides a buffer against muscle loss (sarcopenia) and illness. For children and teenagers, BMI is assessed using age- and sex-specific growth charts (BMI-for-age percentiles) rather than the adult categories, as body composition changes significantly during growth.
