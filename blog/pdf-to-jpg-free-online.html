PDF to JPG: the free, no-upload way to convert

6 min read · Published April 2026

You have a PDF — maybe it's a scanned document, a report with diagrams, a bank statement, a contract — and you need images from it. You search online, find a converter, and then it asks you to upload your file to their server. For anything sensitive, that's a non-starter. Even for routine conversions, you don't know who sees the file, how long they keep it, or what they do with it.

The good news: PDF to JPG conversion can happen entirely in your browser. No upload required. This article explains how, covers the DPI and format trade-offs you need to know, and answers the most common questions about PDF image conversion.

JPG vs PNG vs WebP — which format to choose

JPG

Best for: photos, scanned documents, anything with continuous tones. Pros: small file size, universal compatibility. Cons: lossy compression — some quality loss, not ideal if you need to re-edit the image later. For a typical A4 scanned page at 150 DPI, JPG produces files of 200–600 KB.

PNG

Best for: diagrams, charts, presentations, text-heavy pages, anything where you need pixel-perfect sharpness and no compression artefacts. Pros: lossless, preserves every pixel exactly. Cons: much larger files — the same page that's 400 KB as JPG might be 1–4 MB as PNG. Use PNG when you need to zoom in and read small text clearly.

WebP

Best for: web pages, email, anywhere you're embedding the images digitally. Pros: 25–30% smaller than JPG at the same visual quality. Cons: not supported by all older apps and some print workflows. If your end destination is a website, WebP is the best choice.

DPI explained: what number should you actually use?

DPI (dots per inch) controls the resolution of the output image. Higher DPI = more pixels = larger file = sharper image when printed or zoomed.

DPI A4 page size Best for
72 DPI595 × 842 pxThumbnail, quick preview
96 DPI794 × 1123 pxWeb/email at small display size
150 DPI1240 × 1754 pxGeneral web, social media, email
200 DPI1654 × 2339 pxHigh-res web, legible text zoom
300 DPI2480 × 3508 pxPrinting, archiving, OCR pre-processing

For most use cases — sending a PDF page as an image in WhatsApp, posting a document screenshot on social media, embedding in a presentation — 150 DPI in JPG format is the right choice. It produces a sharp-looking image at a manageable file size.

When your output still looks blurry

If the output image looks blurry or low-resolution even at 300 DPI, the problem is in the source PDF, not the conversion. PDFs contain either vector content (resolution-independent mathematical shapes, which look sharp at any DPI) or raster/embedded images (actual pixels at a fixed resolution). If someone created the PDF by scanning physical paper at 100 DPI and you convert at 300 DPI, the output is 300 DPI of upscaled 100 DPI content — still blurry.

The fix: get a better-quality source file (higher resolution scan, or the original digital PDF rather than a re-scanned version).

Upload-based services: the risks you should know

Popular services like Smallpdf, ILovePDF, Adobe Acrobat Online, and PDF2Doc upload your file to their cloud servers. Most have a 30-day data retention policy (they keep your file for 30 days). Some are governed by GDPR in the EU, but many are not — and most have clauses permitting use of uploaded content for "service improvement."

For a recipe PDF or a novel? Probably fine. For a bank statement, passport scan, signed contract, medical record, or tax document? Use a browser-based tool that never uploads your file.
