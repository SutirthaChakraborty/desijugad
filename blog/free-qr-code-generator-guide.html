Free QR code generator: how QR codes work and when to use them in 2026

10 min read · Updated April 2026

QR codes quietly became essential infrastructure. According to Statista, QR code interactions surpassed 26 billion globally in 2024 and are still growing. Every Indian restaurant table has one. Every UPI payment uses one. Vaccine certificates, boarding passes, event tickets — all QR codes. Yet most people have no idea how to create one, what to put in it, or why some QR codes expire and some don't.

This guide demystifies them completely.

What a QR code actually is

QR stands for Quick Response. Invented by Denso Wave (a Toyota subsidiary) in 1994 to track car parts, it's a 2D matrix barcode that encodes data in a grid of black and white squares. The ISO/IEC 18004 standard governs its specification.

The key properties that make QR codes useful:

  • High data density — a single QR code can hold up to 4,296 alphanumeric characters, 7,089 numeric digits, or 2,953 bytes of binary data.
  • Error correction — QR codes contain redundant data using Reed-Solomon error correction, which means codes are readable even if up to 30% of the image is damaged or obscured. This is why logos can be placed in the centre without breaking functionality.
  • Omnidirectional scanning — unlike 1D barcodes, QR codes can be scanned at any rotation angle.
  • Open standard — Denso Wave holds the patent but has never enforced it, making QR codes royalty-free.

Static vs dynamic QR codes

This is the most important distinction, and most guides gloss over it.

Static QR codes encode data directly in the pattern. The URL or text is baked in. You can never change it after printing. It works forever, with no account needed, and the scan goes directly to the destination without any intermediate service.

Dynamic QR codes encode a short URL that redirects to your actual destination. The visible QR pattern points to something like qr.example.com/abc123, and that redirect target is configurable. This lets you change where the code points after printing. You also get scan analytics — how many scans, from which countries, on which devices.

The catch with dynamic QR codes: they require an ongoing service. If the company providing the redirect goes offline or you stop paying, your printed QR codes break. Several popular free dynamic QR services have shut down, turning thousands of printed menus and billboards into dead links overnight.

For permanent physical print — business cards, product packaging, book covers — use a static QR code encoding a URL you control. For campaigns where you need analytics or the destination might change, use a dynamic code but understand the dependency you're creating.

What data types you can encode

QR codes aren't just for URLs. Common data types:

  • URL — most common. Opens in the default browser.
  • WiFi credentials — format: WIFI:T:WPA;S:NetworkName;P:password;;. Scanning connects the phone to the network automatically.
  • vCard / Contact info — adds a contact to the phone's address book in one tap.
  • Plain text — shows the text in the scanner app.
  • Email — opens a new email compose window with pre-filled recipient and subject.
  • SMS / WhatsApp message — opens a pre-filled message.
  • Location / coordinates — opens maps to a specific latitude/longitude.
  • UPI payment — the backbone of India's payment QR system.
  • Calendar event — adds an event to the calendar with one scan.

How to make a QR code that actually works in print

Most QR codes fail at print because of these preventable mistakes:

  1. Download SVG, not PNG — SVG scales infinitely without pixelation. A PNG QR code that looks sharp at 200px becomes blurry at business-card size when scaled. Always use SVG for anything going to print.
  2. Maintain minimum size — the minimum reliable scan size on paper is about 2 × 2 cm. Smaller than that and many phones won't read it at arm's length.
  3. Keep the quiet zone — the white border around the code is part of the spec. Don't crop it or place the code edge-to-edge in a coloured box.
  4. High contrast only — dark pattern on light background. Reverse (light on dark) technically works but reduces scanner reliability, especially under glare. Gold-on-white or light-blue-on-white are common failure patterns.
  5. Test before printing 1,000 copies — scan with multiple devices and in different lighting conditions before going to print. Use ZXing or simply your phone's native camera app.

QR codes for UPI and payments in India

India's UPI (Unified Payments Interface) uses a specific QR format defined by NPCI. A UPI QR code contains a URI like upi://pay?pa=merchant@upi&pn=MerchantName&am=100&cu=INR. Scanning this in any UPI app (Google Pay, PhonePe, Paytm, BHIM) pre-fills the payment details. Pre-filled amount QR codes work for fixed-price items; amount-less QR codes let the payer enter the amount, suitable for service businesses.

For UPI QR codes, always use a static QR encoding the upi:// URI directly — no redirect services needed, and no monthly fee.
