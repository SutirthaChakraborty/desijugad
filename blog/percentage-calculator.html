Percentage Calculator Online Free — Calculate % Instantly

April 27, 2026 • 4 min read

Percentage calculations appear in nearly every financial decision — from shopping discounts to bank interest to tax bills. Yet surprisingly many people reach for a calculator app and then are not sure which formula to use. This guide covers every common percentage problem with the formula, a worked example, and when each type comes up in real life.

The 3 Types of Percentage Problems

Almost every percentage question falls into one of three categories. Knowing which type you are dealing with immediately tells you which formula to use.

Type 1: What is X% of Y? (Finding a part)

Formula: (X ÷ 100) × Y

Example: What is 18% of ₹5,000? = (18 ÷ 100) × 5,000 = ₹900

Real-life uses: Calculating GST on a purchase, finding the tip amount on a restaurant bill, computing commission on a sale, calculating a discount amount before subtracting it from a price.

Type 2: X is what percentage of Y? (Finding the rate)

Formula: (X ÷ Y) × 100

Example: ₹180 is what percentage of ₹1,000? = (180 ÷ 1,000) × 100 = 18%

Real-life uses: Calculating profit margin, finding your exam score as a percentage, determining market share, working out what fraction of your budget goes to a particular expense.

Type 3: X is Y% of what? (Finding the whole)

Formula: X ÷ (Y ÷ 100)

Example: ₹180 is 18% of what amount? = 180 ÷ 0.18 = ₹1,000

Real-life uses: Finding the original price before a discount was applied, calculating the pre-GST price when you know the GST-inclusive price, working backwards from a known result to the base figure.

Percentage Change (Increase and Decrease)

Formula: ((New Value − Old Value) ÷ Old Value) × 100

A positive result is an increase; a negative result is a decrease.

Example: A product's price went from ₹800 to ₹1,000. Change = ((1,000 − 800) ÷ 800) × 100 = 25% increase.

Common mistake — percentage increases and decreases are not symmetric. A 50% decrease followed by a 50% increase does NOT return to the original value. If you start at ₹1,000 and drop 50%, you reach ₹500. A 50% increase from ₹500 brings you to only ₹750 — not ₹1,000. This asymmetry trips up many people when comparing investment losses and recovery gains.

Percentage Points vs Percentage

This is one of the most misunderstood distinctions in finance and journalism, and confusing the two can significantly overstate or understate a change.

Percentage change describes a relative change from a baseline. Percentage points describe an absolute change in percentage values.

Example: A bank's home loan interest rate rises from 8% to 10%. This is a 2 percentage point increase. But as a relative change, it is a 25% increase in the rate ((10 − 8) ÷ 8 × 100 = 25%).

Why does this matter? A headline saying "interest rates rose 25%" sounds alarming. A headline saying "interest rates rose 2 percentage points" sounds modest. Both describe exactly the same event. Always check which type of change is being reported.

Real-World Applications

  • Adding GST/VAT: Multiply the base price by 1.18 to add 18% GST. Example: ₹2,000 + 18% GST = ₹2,000 × 1.18 = ₹2,360.
  • Removing GST from a GST-inclusive price: Divide by 1.18 — do NOT subtract 18%. Example: A GST-inclusive price of ₹2,360 ÷ 1.18 = ₹2,000 base price. (Subtracting 18% would give the wrong answer: ₹2,360 × 0.82 = ₹1,935.)
  • Shopping discounts: 30% off ₹2,000 = ₹2,000 × (1 − 0.30) = ₹1,400. Or: 30% of ₹2,000 = ₹600 discount, leaving ₹1,400.
  • Bank interest: ₹10,000 at 7% per annum for 1 year = ₹700 simple interest. Total = ₹10,700.
  • Exam scores: 65 marks out of 80 = (65 ÷ 80) × 100 = 81.25%.
  • Salary hike: Current salary ₹50,000. A 15% hike = ₹50,000 × 1.15 = ₹57,500.

Compound vs Simple Percentage Growth

When dealing with growth over multiple periods (investments, loans, inflation), it matters whether the percentage applies to the original amount or to the growing total.

Simple percentage growth: Each period's gain is calculated on the original principal. ₹10,000 at 10% simple growth for 3 years = ₹10,000 + (₹1,000 × 3) = ₹13,000.

Compound growth: Each period's gain is calculated on the new total. ₹10,000 at 10% compound growth for 3 years = ₹10,000 × (1.10)³ = ₹10,000 × 1.331 = ₹13,310.

The difference is small over 3 years but grows dramatically over decades. At 10% for 30 years, simple growth gives ₹40,000 while compound growth gives ₹174,494. This is why compound interest makes long-term investing so powerful — and why high-interest credit card debt (which compounds monthly) is so dangerous to carry.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I calculate the original price before a discount?

Use the reverse percentage formula: Original price = Sale price ÷ (1 − discount rate). Example: An item is on 20% sale for ₹800. Original price = ₹800 ÷ (1 − 0.20) = ₹800 ÷ 0.80 = ₹1,000. This works for any "X% off" scenario — just substitute the discount rate.

What is the difference between percentage increase and percentage points?

Percentage increase is relative — it tells you how much something grew compared to its starting value. Percentage points are absolute — they measure the arithmetic difference between two percentage figures. If a fund's return goes from 4% to 6%, it increased by 2 percentage points (absolute) or by 50% (relative). In everyday financial contexts, percentage points are usually the more honest and useful measure.

How do I add tax (GST/VAT) to a price and remove it?

To add GST: multiply by (1 + tax rate). For 18% GST: price × 1.18. To remove GST from a tax-inclusive price: divide by (1 + tax rate). For 18% GST: inclusive price ÷ 1.18. The common mistake is subtracting 18% to "remove" GST — this gives the wrong answer because 18% of the tax-inclusive price is not the same as 18% of the original base price.
