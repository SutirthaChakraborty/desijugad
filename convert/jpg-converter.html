About the JPG / JPEG format

JPG (also known as JPEG — Joint Photographic Experts Group) is the world's most widely used image format, standardised in 1992. It uses lossy compression: when you save a photo as JPG, the encoder discards subtle colour and detail information that the human eye is unlikely to notice. A quality setting of 80–90 % typically reduces file size by 60–80 % compared to an uncompressed bitmap with almost no visible difference.

JPG is ideal for photographs and complex scenes with gradients and millions of colours. It is a poor choice for screenshots, logos and graphics with flat colours or sharp edges — those artifacts become visible. For those use cases, PNG or SVG are better choices. JPG does not support transparency; transparent areas will be filled with white when encoding.

Every time you open and re-save a JPG, additional compression artifacts accumulate. If you plan to edit an image repeatedly, work in PNG or TIF and export to JPG only as the final delivery format.

FAQ

What is the difference between JPG and JPEG?

JPG and JPEG are exactly the same format. JPEG stands for Joint Photographic Experts Group. The extension was shortened to JPG on early Windows systems which limited file extensions to 3 characters. Both extensions refer to identical image data — you can rename .jpeg to .jpg without any change to the file contents.

Does converting JPG to PNG improve image quality?

No. Once a JPG has been compressed, the lost detail cannot be recovered by converting to PNG. Converting to PNG gives you a lossless container that prevents further quality loss on future re-saves, but it will not restore what the original JPG compression removed. Convert to PNG when you need transparency or want to edit and re-save without cumulative loss.

What is the best format to convert JPG to for websites?

WebP is the best modern replacement for JPG on the web. It achieves 25–35 % smaller file sizes at equivalent visual quality and is supported by all major browsers (Chrome, Firefox, Edge, Safari 14+). For cutting-edge performance, AVIF is even smaller but has slightly lower browser support as of 2026.
