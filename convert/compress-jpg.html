How JPG compression works

JPEG compression uses the Discrete Cosine Transform (DCT) to convert image blocks into frequency components, then quantizes those components — discarding fine detail that is less perceptible to the human eye. This is why JPEG is called "lossy."

The quality slider controls how aggressively the quantization table discards detail:

  • 90–100% — Nearly lossless, large files. Best for printing or archival.
  • 75–85% — Visually indistinguishable from original. Standard for web photography.
  • 60–75% — Small files, minor artifacts on sharp edges. Good for thumbnails.
  • Below 50% — Visible block artifacts (mosquito noise). Only for very small file targets.

JPG compression use cases

  • Passport photos — Most government portals require images under 50–100 KB. Set quality to 65–70%.
  • Website images — Compress to 75–80% to speed up page load without visible quality change.
  • Email attachments — Keep photos under 1 MB to avoid Gmail/Outlook size warnings.
  • WhatsApp / social media — Compress first at 80% to prevent the platform from doing it worse.

FAQ

What quality setting should I use for WhatsApp?

WhatsApp compresses images further when you send them. To retain quality, compress to 80% quality locally and resize to 1280px wide first. This gives you full control over the final result rather than letting WhatsApp decide.

Can I compress a JPG to exactly 50KB?

Use the "Target size (KB)" field. The tool will iteratively reduce quality until the output is at or below your target. Results vary by image content — highly detailed photos need lower quality to hit small targets.

Does compressing a JPG multiple times damage quality?

Yes — each re-save at less than 100% quality adds generation loss. Repeated compression can introduce visible block artifacts. Always keep your original file and compress from the original, not from a previously compressed copy.

Are my images uploaded to a server?

No. All compression runs in JavaScript in your browser using the Canvas API. Nothing is sent to any server.
