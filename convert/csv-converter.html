Why CSV format matters

CSV is not one format — it's a family. European software (Excel in German locale, SAP, many ERP systems) uses semicolons as separators because commas are used as decimal points. Scientific databases use tabs. Log files use pipes. Importing data between systems often means converting the separator first.

Auto-detect

The auto-detect mode scans the first line of your CSV and counts comma, semicolon, tab, and pipe characters. The one that appears most consistently across rows is selected as the delimiter. Works correctly for 95% of real-world files.

Quoted fields

This parser correctly handles RFC 4180 quoting rules. A field like "New Delhi, India" won't be split at the comma — the quotes protect it. Output fields that contain the output delimiter are automatically re-quoted.

FAQ

Will quotes around fields be handled correctly?

Yes. The parser follows RFC 4180 — fields enclosed in double quotes are treated as single values even if they contain the delimiter character. Escaped quotes ("" inside quoted fields) are also handled.

What happens to newlines inside fields?

Fields containing newlines should be properly quoted. The parser handles multi-line quoted fields. If your CSV uses bare newlines inside unquoted fields, results may be unexpected.

How large a CSV can I paste?

There's no hard limit — the entire conversion runs in your browser using pure JavaScript. Files up to tens of MB paste and convert quickly.

Can I use this to convert a European CSV (semicolons) for Excel?

Yes — set input to Semicolon and output to CSV (comma). Alternatively, Excel can open semicolon CSVs if your regional settings use semicolons — this tool gives you control regardless of OS locale.
