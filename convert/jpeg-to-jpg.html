JPEG vs JPG — what is the difference?

Absolutely nothing, from a technical standpoint. Both .jpeg and .jpg refer to the same image compression standard — JPEG (Joint Photographic Experts Group), developed in 1992.

The reason two extensions exist is historical: MS-DOS and early Windows imposed a 3-character limit on file extensions. So the 4-letter .jpeg had to be shortened to .jpg on Windows systems. macOS, Linux, and Unix always supported longer extensions, so .jpeg was used there. Over time, .jpg became the universal standard, but both remain valid.

FAQ

Is JPEG lossy or lossless?

JPEG is a lossy compression format — it discards some image data to achieve smaller file sizes. Each re-save at less than 100% quality results in a small additional quality loss. For archival, use PNG (lossless) instead.

Can I convert JPG to PNG for better quality?

You can, but it won't recover quality already lost during JPEG compression. Converting JPG to PNG simply re-saves it without additional loss. To avoid JPEG artifacts entirely, start from a RAW or PNG source.

Are my images uploaded to a server?
