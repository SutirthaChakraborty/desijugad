When to use MP3 to WAV

WAV (PCM) is the universal format for professional audio work. Importing MP3 directly into some tools causes issues — the decoder overhead, the re-compression cycle, or simple incompatibility. Converting to WAV first solves all of these:

  • Video editing — Adobe Premiere Pro, DaVinci Resolve, and Final Cut work best with PCM WAV audio tracks.
  • DAW workflows — FL Studio, Logic Pro, Ableton Live import WAV with zero latency overhead.
  • Broadcast delivery — TV and radio broadcasters require PCM WAV, often at 48 kHz.
  • Sample libraries — Plugin samplers expect WAV/AIFF, not MP3.

Output file sizes: A 3-minute stereo MP3 (~3 MB) becomes a ~30 MB WAV at 44.1 kHz 16-bit stereo. At 48 kHz it's ~33 MB. Plan your storage accordingly.

FAQ

Will the WAV sound better than the MP3?

No. MP3 is lossy — the quality was reduced when the MP3 was created. Converting to WAV puts those decoded samples into an uncompressed container, but the original lossy compression is already "baked in." The WAV will sound identical to the MP3 playback.

Which sample rate should I choose?

44,100 Hz is CD quality and works everywhere. 48,000 Hz is the standard for video/broadcast — use this for Premiere, DaVinci Resolve, or any video work. 22,050 Hz is for telephony or when small file size matters more.

Can I convert other formats to WAV too?

Yes! The file input accepts any audio format your browser can decode: OGG, M4A, FLAC, AAC, and more. All will be output as 16-bit PCM WAV at your chosen sample rate.

How big will the WAV file be?

WAV size = sample rate × channels × 2 bytes × duration in seconds. A 5-minute stereo file at 44.1 kHz = 44100 × 2 × 2 × 300 = ~50 MB. At 48 kHz = ~55 MB.
