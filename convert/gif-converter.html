About the GIF format

GIF (Graphics Interchange Format) was introduced by CompuServe in 1987 — before the World Wide Web existed. Despite its age, it remains ubiquitous as the format of choice for short, looping animations shared across social media, messaging apps, and reaction libraries.

GIF uses the LZW lossless compression algorithm but is restricted to a maximum palette of 256 colours per frame. This limitation makes GIF poorly suited for photographs or any content with smooth gradients, producing visible colour banding and dithering artifacts. It does support binary transparency (a pixel is either transparent or opaque, with no partial transparency), which made it the original format for web graphics with transparent backgrounds before PNG took over that role.

GIF animations work by storing a sequence of still frames with per-frame delays. There is no video compression between frames — each frame is independently compressed — making animated GIFs enormous compared to equivalent video. A 5-second GIF can easily be 10–20 MB, while the same clip as H.264 MP4 might be under 500 KB. For performance-sensitive websites, convert GIFs to MP4 and use the HTML <video autoplay loop muted playsinline> pattern.

FAQ

Why should I convert GIF to MP4?

MP4 video files are typically 5–20x smaller than equivalent GIF animations. GIFs are limited to 256 colours per frame, which causes visible banding and dithering. MP4 uses modern H.264 or H.265 compression with millions of colours, producing much smoother, smaller files. Most modern platforms (Twitter, Slack, Discord, etc.) silently convert GIF uploads to MP4 themselves.

How many colours does GIF support?

GIF is limited to a palette of 256 colours per frame. This is a fundamental limitation of the format, dating back to its 1987 origin. Photographs and video frames with millions of colours are heavily degraded when encoded as GIF. WebP animated images overcome this limitation while keeping web compatibility.

What is the best alternative to GIF for animations?

For web use: animated WebP (lossless, smaller, full colour) or MP4/AVI video with autoplay and loop attributes. For social sharing: MP4 is universally accepted. For stickers and messaging apps: APNG (animated PNG) or WebP. GIF remains useful only where legacy compatibility is strictly required.
