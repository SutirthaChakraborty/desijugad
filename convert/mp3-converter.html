About the MP3 format

MP3 (MPEG-1 Audio Layer III) was developed in Germany at the Fraunhofer Society and standardised in 1993. It became the defining audio format of the digital music era, powering the iPod, early music downloads, and the first generation of internet radio.

MP3 achieves small file sizes through perceptual (lossy) compression. The encoder analyses audio and discards frequencies and sounds that psychoacoustic models predict the human ear will not notice — for example, sounds masked by a louder simultaneous sound. The degree of compression is controlled by the bitrate:

  • 128 kbps — compact, fine for speech and podcasts.
  • 192 kbps — good general quality, most listeners find it transparent.
  • 320 kbps — maximum standard MP3 quality, used for music distribution.
  • VBR (Variable Bitrate) — adjusts bitrate dynamically, best size-to-quality ratio.

MP3 remains universally supported across every device and platform, from smart speakers and car stereos to every desktop OS and mobile device. More modern codecs like AAC (M4A), Opus, and FLAC offer better quality or lossless options but do not enjoy the same universal legacy support.

FAQ

What bitrate should I use when converting to MP3?

128 kbps is acceptable for speech and podcasts. 192 kbps is a good general-purpose setting that most people find indistinguishable from the source. 320 kbps is the highest standard MP3 bitrate, suitable for music where audio quality matters. Variable bitrate (VBR) mode at quality level V0 or V2 gives excellent quality with smaller files than constant 320 kbps.

Does converting to MP3 lose quality from the original?

Yes. MP3 is a lossy format — converting to MP3 permanently removes audio data that the encoder deems inaudible. If your source is already an MP3, re-encoding to another MP3 compounds the quality loss. Whenever possible, keep an uncompressed original (WAV or FLAC) and export MP3 only for distribution.

What is the difference between M4A and MP3?

M4A uses the AAC audio codec inside an MPEG-4 container. AAC is technically superior to MP3 — it achieves better quality at the same bitrate or equivalent quality at a lower bitrate. M4A files are commonly produced by Apple devices and iTunes. MP3 has broader compatibility with older devices and software. Both are lossy formats.
