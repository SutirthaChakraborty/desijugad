About GIF format

GIF (Graphics Interchange Format) was invented in 1987 and remains the most universally supported animated image format. It supports up to 256 colors per frame and lossless per-frame compression. This makes it ideal for flat-color animations, logos, and simple illustrations, but it looks pixelated for photo sequences.

GIF tips for best results

  • Resize your frames — Keep GIF width at 400–600px. GIFs with high-resolution frames are very large.
  • Limit the palette — Images with fewer distinct colors produce much smaller GIFs.
  • Frame delay — For a smooth animation, 40–80ms (12–25fps) works well. For slides, use 1000–2000ms.
  • Loop forever for memes — Most GIF memes are set to loop forever (0 = infinite).

FAQ

Why is my GIF so large in file size?

GIF file size depends on image dimensions, number of frames, and color complexity. Resize frames to 480px wide and use images with simple flat colors to minimize file size. A 10-frame, 480px animation should be under 1MB.

Can I reorder frames after uploading?

Yes — drag and drop the frame thumbnails to reorder them before clicking Create GIF.

Are there alternatives to GIF for animations?

Yes. Animated WebP and APNG support millions of colors and are smaller than GIF. MP4/WebM video is even better for long animations. However, GIF remains the most universally compatible format for sharing on social media and messaging apps.
